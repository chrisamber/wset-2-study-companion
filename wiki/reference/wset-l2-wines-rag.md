# WSET Level 2 Award in Wines — RAG Corpus

Retrieval-oriented reference covering the full WSET L2 syllabus. Each section is a self-contained chunk. Authoritative scope traces to `raw/articles/wset/wset-l2-wines-spec.md`; tasting descriptors are synthesised study aids (the spec defines *which* attributes are assessed, not their canonical values).

---

## LO1 — Vineyard: grape, vine, ripening, climate

**Parts of a grape and what they give the winemaker:** Skin → colour, tannins, flavours. Pulp → sugar, acids, water, flavours. Seeds and stems → tannins.

**A vine needs:** warmth, sunlight, water, nutrients, and carbon dioxide (CO₂).

**Grape formation & ripening:** flowering → fruit set → véraison (colour change, ripening begins). Ripeness stages: unripe → ripe → extra-ripe (late harvest, raisined). As grapes ripen: sugar rises, acid falls, colour and tannins develop; aromas shift from green/herbaceous toward riper fruit (black grapes redder→darker fruit; white grapes greener→tropical).

**Climate categories:** cool, moderate, warm. **Other climatic influences:** latitude, altitude, mountains, slope, aspect, soils, seas, rivers, air, fog, cloud, mist. **Weather:** drought, frost, hail, rain, sunlight, temperature.

**Grape-growing options:** training, pruning, irrigation, spraying, organic production, yield management, harvest timing. **Concentrating grape sugars:** leaving grapes extra-ripe, encouraging botrytis (noble rot), or freezing grapes (for icewine).

## LO1 — Geographical Indications (GIs) & origin labelling

A **Geographical Indication (GI)** ties a wine to a defined place with rules. EU framework:
- **PDO (Protected Designation of Origin)** — tighter, smaller, place-specific rules.
- **PGI (Protected Geographical Indication)** — broader area, looser rules.

National hierarchies (high quality/tight → broad):
- **France:** AOC / AOP (= PDO) ; Vin de Pays (VdP) / IGP (= PGI).
- **Italy:** DOCG → DOC (= PDO) ; IGT (= PGI).
- **Spain:** DOCa → DO (= PDO) ; Vino de la Tierra (= PGI).
- **Germany:** Qualitätswein, Prädikatswein (= PDO) ; Landwein (= PGI).
- **Outside the EU:** wines use their own GIs.

**Vine-age & grape-growing labelling terms:** Old Vine / Vieilles Vignes; Vintage; Late Harvest / Vendanges Tardives; Botrytis / Noble Rot; Icewine / Eiswein.

## LO2 — Winemaking & bottle ageing

**Alcoholic fermentation:** yeast converts grape sugar into alcohol + carbon dioxide.

**Winemaking process stages:** crushing → fermentation → pressing → storage and/or maturation → blending → packaging.

**Red vs white vs rosé:** Red ferments *with* skins (extraction of colour/tannin). White ferments *without* skins (or brief contact). Rosé: short skin maceration, or blending.

**Winemaking options that shape style:**
- Adjustments: add sugar (chaptalisation), adjust acidity.
- Fermentation temperature: cool (preserves fresh fruity aromas, typical for whites) vs warm (extracts more, typical for reds).
- Sweet wine: stop fermentation early, or add sweetness.
- Vessels: stainless steel / concrete (neutral) vs oak. Oak vessels vary by size (small/large), age (new/old), and level of toast — new small oak gives most flavour (vanilla, spice, toast).
- **Malolactic conversion (MLC):** sharp malic acid → softer lactic acid (buttery; common in reds and full-bodied whites like Chardonnay).
- **Lees ageing:** time on dead yeast cells adds body and bready/savoury complexity.
- **Blending:** for consistency, complexity, or style.

**Bottle ageing:** Red → colour shifts ruby→garnet/brown, tannins soften, fresh fruit → dried fruit/leather/earth. White → colour deepens toward gold, fresh fruit → honey/nutty notes.

## LO3 — The eight principal grape varieties

Characteristics tracked: colour, tannin level (black grapes), sugar/potential alcohol, acidity, aromas & flavours.

**Black (principal):**
- **Merlot** — medium–high tannin, medium–high alcohol; red plum/blackberry, soft; Bordeaux blends.
- **Cabernet Sauvignon** — high tannin, high alcohol, high acid; blackcurrant, green bell pepper (less ripe), cedar with oak; ages long.
- **Syrah/Shiraz** — high tannin; blackberry, black pepper, smoke (cool climate) → riper/spicier jammy fruit (warm, e.g. Barossa).
- **Pinot Noir** — low–medium tannin, high acid; red cherry/strawberry, earthy; delicate.

**White (principal):**
- **Chardonnay** — dry; lean/citrus & mineral unoaked (Chablis) → ripe stone/tropical fruit + oak & MLC (warm regions, oaked).
- **Pinot Grigio/Gris** — light, neutral, citrus (Italy, Grigio) → fuller, riper, spiced (Alsace, Gris).
- **Riesling** — high acid; floral/citrus/stone fruit, petrol with age; dry → lusciously sweet.
- **Sauvignon Blanc** — high acid; gooseberry, grass, green herbs, citrus; aromatic, usually unoaked.

## LO4 — Regionally important black-grape varieties (variety → GIs → terms)

- **GAMAY** — France: Beaujolais, Beaujolais Villages, Beaujolais Cru (e.g. Fleurie). Light, low tannin, high acid, red fruit.
- **GRENACHE/GARNACHA** — France Southern Rhône: Côtes du Rhône, Côtes du Rhône Villages, Châteauneuf-du-Pape; South of France: Minervois. Spain: Rioja, Navarra, Priorat. Australia: Barossa Valley, McLaren Vale. Ripe red fruit, high alcohol, soft tannin.
- **TEMPRANILLO** — Spain: Rioja, Ribera del Duero, Catalunya. Terms: **Joven, Crianza, Reserva, Gran Reserva** (ageing ladder). Leather, strawberry, tobacco.
- **NEBBIOLO** — Italy: Barolo, Barbaresco. High tannin + high acid; rose, tar, cherry.
- **BARBERA** — Italy: Barbera d'Asti. High acid, low–medium tannin, juicy red fruit.
- **SANGIOVESE** — Italy: Chianti, Chianti Classico, Brunello di Montalcino. Terms: **Classico, Riserva**. High acid, savoury cherry, herbal.
- **CORVINA** — Italy: Valpolicella, Valpolicella Classico. Terms: **Classico, Amarone, Recioto** (Amarone = dried-grape dry; Recioto = dried-grape sweet).
- **MONTEPULCIANO** — Italy: Montepulciano d'Abruzzo (the grape, not the Tuscan town).
- **ZINFANDEL/PRIMITIVO** — USA: California; Italy: Puglia. Ripe jammy fruit, high alcohol.
- **PINOTAGE** — South Africa: Western Cape. Term: **Cape Blend**.
- **CARMENÈRE** — Chile: Central Valley. Herbaceous, red/black fruit.
- **MALBEC** — Argentina: Mendoza. Deep colour, plum/blackberry, smooth.

## LO5 — Sparkling wines

**Grapes used for sparkling:** Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, Meunier, Moscato (Muscat), Glera.

**Methods:**
- **Traditional method** — second fermentation *in the bottle*; gives fine persistent bubbles and bready/autolytic (lees) complexity. Used for **Champagne** (France) and **Cava** (Spain). South Africa's version is labelled **Méthode Cap Classique**.
- **Tank method (Charmat)** — second fermentation in a *pressurised tank*; preserves fresh primary fruit/floral aromas. Used for **Prosecco** (Glera) and **Asti** (Moscato). Made in dry and sweet styles.

**GIs:** France — Champagne; Italy — Asti, Prosecco; Spain — Cava; also South Africa, USA, Australia, New Zealand.
**Labelling terms:** Brut (dry), Demi-Sec (medium-sweet), Vintage / Non-Vintage (NV), Traditional Method, Méthode Cap Classique.

## LO5 — Fortified wines (Sherry vs Port)

Fortification = adding grape spirit, raising ABV. **The timing of fortification is the key distinction:**
- **Sherry (Spain, Jerez)** — fortified *after* fermentation finishes, so the base is **dry**. Then aged either **under flor** (a yeast veil → fresh, saline, e.g. Fino) or **oxidatively** (in contact with oxygen → nutty, e.g. Oloroso). Styles/terms: **Fino, Amontillado, Oloroso, Pale Cream, Medium, Cream, PX (Pedro Ximénez)** (PX = intensely sweet, raisined).
- **Port (Portugal, Douro)** — fortified *to interrupt* fermentation, leaving residual **sweetness**, then aged. Terms: **Ruby, Reserve Ruby, Late Bottled Vintage (LBV), Vintage, Tawny** (Tawny = long oxidative cask ageing → dried-fruit, nutty, brown-tinged).

## LO6 — Storage & preservation

**Ideal storage conditions:** cool, *constant* temperature; minimal light; bottle on its side (keeps cork moist — for cork-sealed bottles). Avoid heat and temperature swings.
**Preserving opened wine:** vacuum system (pump out air) or blanket system (inert gas over the wine).

---

## LO6 — Service temperatures

- **Sparkling wines** — well chilled.
- **Sweet wines** — well chilled.
- **Light- and medium-bodied white & rosé** — chilled.
- **Full-bodied white** — lightly chilled.
- **Light-bodied red** — room temperature or lightly chilled.
- **Medium- and full-bodied red** — room temperature.

(Chilling raises perceived acidity/tannin and suppresses fruit/sweetness; warmth raises perceived alcohol and aromatics. See [[wine-serving-temperature]].)

---

## LO6 — Opening, serving & faults

**Serving:** correct opening of still vs sparkling wine; decanting (for sediment or aeration); appropriate glassware; check appearance and nose before serving.
**Common faults:**
- **Cork taint (TCA)** — musty, wet-cardboard smell; from contaminated cork.
- **Failure of closure** — lets in air → premature oxidation.
- **Heat damage** — wine cooked by high storage temperatures; flat, stewed character.

---

## LO6 — Food & wine interactions

**Components in food that affect how wine tastes:** sweetness, umami, acid, salt, flavour intensity, fat, chilli heat.
**Components in wine affected by food:** sweetness, acidity, bitterness (from tannins, oak), fruitiness, alcohol.

Key effects (see [[wine-food-pairing]] for the L1 principles): sweet/umami food makes wine seem more bitter, acidic, less fruity (harder); salt and acid in food make wine seem softer, sweeter, more fruity (easier). Match the **weight** of food and wine; use acidity to cut fat; pair tannin with protein/fat; the food should not be sweeter than the wine.

## Exam structure (for self-testing)

50 MCQs, 60 minutes, closed-book, 1 mark each, no negative marking; **pass = 55%** (Merit ≥ 70%, Distinction ≥ 85%). Question weighting: LO1 = 5, LO2 = 4, **LO3 = 19, LO4 = 12**, LO5 = 6, LO6 = 4. Concentrate study on grape varieties, their regions/GIs, and labelling terms (LO3 + LO4 = 62% of the exam).
