# WSET Level 2 Award in Wines

## Analogy

If [[wine-types|WSET Level 1]] teaches you the *grammar* of wine — that there are reds, whites, sparkling, fortified, and a handful of structural components — then **Level 2 teaches the vocabulary and geography**: which grape varieties exist, what they taste like, *where* they're grown, and how a label encodes all of that. L1 lets you describe a glass; L2 lets you read a wall of bottles and predict what's inside each one.

## Mechanics

The qualification is defined by **six Learning Outcomes (LOs)**, and the exam weighting tells you exactly where to spend study time.

### The six Learning Outcomes
1. **Vineyard** — what a grape and a vine need; ripening (flowering → fruit set → véraison); climate (cool/moderate/warm) and weather; grape-growing options; origin/regulation labelling (GIs).
2. **Winemaking & bottle ageing** — fermentation (yeast → alcohol + CO₂); red/rosé/white processes; options (temperature, oak, malolactic conversion, lees, blending); how bottle age changes colour/tannin/aroma.
3. **Principal grape varieties** — the eight headline varieties, their characteristics, and their geographical indications.
4. **Regionally important varieties** — ~22 further varieties with their key regions and labelling terms.
5. **Sparkling & fortified** — traditional vs tank method; Sherry vs Port; their GIs and labelling terms.
6. **Storage, service & food pairing** — conditions, service temperatures, faults, and food↔wine interactions.

### Exam weighting — study where the marks are
50 MCQs, 60 minutes, pass at 55%:

| LO | Topic | Questions |
|----|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Vineyard | 5 |
| 2 | Winemaking & ageing | 4 |
| **3** | **Principal varieties + GIs** | **19** |
| **4** | **Regional varieties + GIs** | **12** |
| 5 | Sparkling & fortified | 6 |
| 6 | Storage / service / pairing | 4 |

**LO3 + LO4 = 31/50 (62%).** Grape varieties and *where they're grown* are the exam.

### The principal grape varieties (LO3)
- **Black:** Merlot, Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah/Shiraz, Pinot Noir.
- **White:** Chardonnay, Pinot Grigio/Gris, Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc.

Each is tracked across colour, tannin (black), sugar/potential alcohol, acidity, and aroma/flavour, then mapped to PGI/PDO GIs across France, Italy, Spain, Germany and the New World (USA, Chile, South Africa, Australia, New Zealand). The full variety→region tables live in the [[wset-l2-wines-rag|RAG corpus]].

### The labelling/GI framework
L2's spine is being able to interpret a label. EU **PDO** (tighter, place-specific) vs **PGI** (broader); the national hierarchies — France AOC/AOP, Italy DOCG/DOC/IGT, Spain DOCa/DO, Germany Qualitätswein/Prädikatswein; and quality/ageing terms (Rioja's Crianza→Gran Reserva, Italy's Classico/Riserva, Burgundy/Alsace Premier/Grand Cru, Germany's Kabinett→Trockenbeerenauslese).

### Sparkling & fortified (LO5)
- **Sparkling:** *traditional method* (second fermentation in bottle → Champagne, Cava) vs *tank method* (Charmat → Prosecco, Asti). Labels: Brut, Demi-Sec, Vintage/NV, Méthode Cap Classique.
- **Fortified — the key contrast:** **Sherry** is fortified *after* fermentation (dry base; flor or oxidative ageing → Fino…Oloroso, PX), while **Port** is fortified *to interrupt* fermentation, leaving sweetness (Ruby, LBV, Vintage, Tawny).

## Implications

- **Where it sits:** L1 → **L2** → L3 → L4 Diploma. L2 (or equivalent) is the recommended entry to L3. TQT 28 h (17 GLH + 11 private study). No entry restrictions; offered only through Approved Programme Providers (APPs); assessed by WSET Awards (Ofqual-recognised).
- **Study strategy:** because LO3/LO4 dominate, mastering the variety↔region↔labelling-term tables is the single highest-leverage activity — far more than the vineyard/winemaking theory L1 students often over-study.
- **Practical payoff:** the stated aim is reading labels and giving basic selection/service guidance — exactly the hospitality/retail use case.

## Misconceptions

- **"L2 is just more of L1."** No — L1 is style categories and structure; L2 adds *named varieties, named regions, and the GI/label system*. The depth jump is in geography and labelling, not theory.
- **"Sherry and Port differ mainly in sweetness."** The *cause* is fortification timing — Port is fortified mid-fermentation (sugar remains), Sherry after (base is dry); ageing style (flor/oxidative) then shapes the rest.
- **"Vineyard and winemaking are the bulk of the exam."** They're only 9 of 50 marks combined; varieties and regions are 31.
- **"It's an open-book or tasting exam."** It's a *closed-book, 50-question MCQ* paper; tasting is recommended for learning but not examined at L2.
