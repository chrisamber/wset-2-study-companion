# Wine and Food Pairing (WSET Level 1)

## Analogy

Pairing wine and food is like mixing two audio signals: you want them to either complement each other (similar frequencies reinforce) or contrast cleanly (different frequencies occupy separate space). Fat in food absorbs tannin the way a compressor absorbs transients — the impact is controlled and the result feels smoother. Acidity in wine acts like a high-pass filter cutting through richness, keeping the overall sound (meal) bright. Clash is when both signals fight over the same frequency: tannic red with delicate fish, for example.

## Mechanics

### The four core principles (WSET 1)

#### 1. Match weight / body

The body of the wine should match the weight of the dish. Light wine with delicate food; full-bodied wine with rich food.

| Food weight | Wine to match |
|-------------|--------------|
| Delicate (sole, goat's cheese, salads) | Light white (Pinot Grigio, Muscadet) |
| Medium (roast chicken, pork, pasta with cream) | Medium-bodied white or light-medium red (Chardonnay, Pinot Noir) |
| Rich (red meat, game, aged hard cheese) | Full-bodied red (Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah, Barolo) |

#### 2. Match / contrast acidity

- **High acidity in wine** cuts through fat and richness → refreshes the palate
- Oily or fried food + acidic/sparkling wine = classic match (fish and chips + Champagne; calamari + Vermentino)
- **Avoid** low-acidity wine with fatty food — the wine will taste flat and the food heavy

#### 3. Match tannin with protein/fat

- Tannins bind to proteins and fats → red wine + red meat softens tannin grip and makes both taste smoother
- **Avoid** high-tannin wine with:
  - Oily fish (mackerel, sardines) — metallic, fishy aftertaste
  - Eggs — tannin turns harsh
  - Delicate white fish — overwhelms the food

#### 4. Match sweetness levels

- Sweet wine with sweet food: the food should never be sweeter than the wine or the wine tastes sour and thin
- Off-dry or sweet wine with spicy food (Thai, Sichuan, Indian) — sweetness cools heat, acidity refreshes
- Port / Sauternes with blue cheese — salt in cheese and sweetness in wine complement each other (salt + sweet effect)

### Classic pairings by wine type

| Wine | Classic food pairings | Why it works |
|------|----------------------|--------------|
| Champagne / sparkling | Fried food, oysters, canapés, sushi | Acidity + bubbles cut oil/fat; light body matches delicate food |
| Sauvignon Blanc | Goat's cheese, asparagus, green herb dishes, salads | Herbaceous notes mirror food; high acidity matches tangy cheese |
| Unoaked Chardonnay / Chablis | Oysters, shellfish, light fish | High acidity, mineral — classic Chablis + oysters pairing |
| Oaked Chardonnay | Lobster, crab, creamy pasta, roast chicken | Full body + oak spice mirrors richness of cream/butter sauces |
| Riesling (off-dry) | Spicy Thai/Indian/Chinese, pork belly, duck | Sweetness cools heat; acidity cuts fat |
| Pinot Grigio | Light pasta, seafood risotto, antipasti | Neutral profile doesn't compete; crisp acidity refreshes |
| Pinot Noir | Duck, salmon, mushroom dishes, charcuterie | Low tannin won't overpower; earthy notes match umami |
| Merlot | Roast pork, veal, pasta bolognese, pizza | Soft tannins, plum fruit — broadly versatile |
| Cabernet Sauvignon | Lamb, beef, aged hard cheese (Cheddar, Manchego) | High tannin + fat/protein of red meat = mutual softening |
| Syrah / Shiraz | BBQ, game, smoked meats, black olive dishes | Bold fruit + pepper echo charred/smoky flavours |
| Sauternes / sweet whites | Foie gras, blue cheese (Roquefort), fruit tarts | Salt of cheese + fat of foie gras balanced by sweetness |
| Port | Dark chocolate, Stilton, walnuts, Christmas cake | Sweetness + richness pair; tannin in Port matches protein in cheese |
| Fino / Manzanilla Sherry | Almonds, olives, jamón, seafood tapas | Bone-dry, saline, yeasty — mirrors salty/savory snacks |

### Pairing failures to avoid

| Combination | Problem |
|-------------|---------|
| Tannic red + oily fish | Wine tastes metallic/fishy |
| Sweet wine + savoury main | Wine tastes cloying/out of place |
| Dessert sweeter than the wine | Wine tastes acidic and thin |
| High-alcohol wine + spicy food | Alcohol amplifies capsaicin burn |
| Oaked Chardonnay + asparagus/artichoke | Wine turns bitter; green veg clash with oak |
| Dry red + vinaigrette-dressed salad | Acidity of dressing clashes with wine's fruit |

### The "anything goes" versatile wines

Some wines pair broadly because of their particular balance of acidity, body, and flavour neutrality:

- **Champagne / dry sparkling** — the universal starter/canapé wine
- **Dry Riesling or Alsatian Pinot Blanc** — food-friendly acidity without strong flavour
- **Pinot Noir** — low tannin works with both white and red meat
- **Dry Fino Sherry** — one of the most food-versatile wines that exists; pairs with almost any savoury food

## Implications

- The weight-matching principle handles ~70% of pairing decisions without needing specific knowledge.
- Regional cuisines often developed alongside local wines for centuries — defaulting to local wine with local food (Sancerre + Loire valley goat's cheese; Barolo + Piedmontese beef) is almost always correct.
- Vegetarian food is often harder to pair than meat because umami and protein (which soften tannins) are reduced; lean towards whites and light reds.

## Misconceptions

- **"Red with red meat, white with fish"** is a useful heuristic but not a rule. Salmon pairs well with Pinot Noir; rich fish dishes can take oaked Chardonnay; tuna steak pairs with light reds.
- **"Cheap wine doesn't matter with food."** Food actually *hides* flaws — this is true. A flawed but affordable wine can seem perfectly fine when paired well.
- **"Spicy food needs beer."** Off-dry Riesling, Gewurztraminer, or Viognier often outperform beer with spicy food.
- **"Dessert wine is just for dessert."** Sauternes with foie gras as a starter is considered one of the world's great pairings.
