# Wine Types (WSET Level 1)

## Analogy

Think of wine like a spectrum from still water to sparkling water — then add the idea of fortification (spirits added, like adding a shot to your drink) and sweetness (residual sugar left in). The grape variety is the "instrument"; the winemaker is the arranger; the region is the acoustic of the room.

## Mechanics

### The four main categories

**Still wines** — no dissolved CO₂; the everyday category.

| Colour | How it's made | Key characteristics |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|
| Red | Fermented with skins on → extracts tannin, colour, flavour | Tannin, structure, darker fruit |
| White | Fermented without skins (or brief contact) | Acidity-driven, lighter body |
| Rosé | Either brief skin contact, or blending red + white (mainly Champagne) | Light tannin, fresh fruit, pink hue |

**Sparkling wines** — CO₂ dissolved under pressure, gives bubbles.

| Wine | Region | Method |
|------|--------|--------|
| Champagne | Champagne, France | Traditional method (second fermentation in bottle) |
| Cava | Spain | Traditional method |
| Prosecco | Veneto, Italy | Tank method (Charmat) — fruitier, less bready |
| Crémant | Various France | Traditional method |

**Fortified wines** — grape spirit added during or after fermentation, raises ABV to ~15–22%.

| Wine | Origin | Style |
|------|--------|-------|
| Port | Douro, Portugal | Spirit added mid-fermentation → sweet, red or white |
| Sherry | Jerez, Spain | Dry (Fino, Manzanilla, Amontillado) or sweet (Pedro Ximénez, Cream) |
| Madeira | Madeira, Portugal | Heated during aging (estufagem) → nutty, long-lived |
| Marsala | Sicily, Italy | Dry to sweet; cooking or drinking |

**Sweet (dessert) wines** — residual sugar retained, via late harvest, noble rot (*Botrytis cinerea*), or drying grapes.

| Wine | Method |
|------|--------|
| Sauternes (France) | Noble rot concentrates sugar |
| Riesling Auslese/TBA (Germany) | Late harvest / noble rot |
| Vin Santo (Italy) | Dried grapes (passito method) |
| Ice wine / Eiswein | Grapes frozen on the vine |

### Sweetness scale (WSET 1 terminology)

Dry → Off-dry → Medium-dry → Medium-sweet → Sweet → Luscious

A "dry" wine has had virtually all sugar fermented to alcohol. "Off-dry" has just perceptible sweetness (e.g. Mosel Kabinett Riesling).

### Key grape varieties

**Red grapes:**
- **Cabernet Sauvignon** — full body, high tannin, blackcurrant, cedar; ages well
- **Merlot** — medium–full body, softer tannin, plum, chocolate; more approachable
- **Pinot Noir** — light–medium body, low tannin, red cherry, earthy; silky
- **Syrah / Shiraz** — full body, high tannin, dark fruit, pepper, smoke
- **Malbec** — medium–full body, plum, violet; juicy in Argentina
- **Tempranillo** — medium body, leather, strawberry, tobacco; core of Rioja

**White grapes:**
- **Chardonnay** — versatile; lean (Chablis) to full-bodied oaked (Burgundy, Napa)
- **Sauvignon Blanc** — high acidity, grassy/herbaceous, gooseberry, citrus
- **Riesling** — high acidity, petrol/floral, ranges dry to sweet; ages very well
- **Pinot Grigio / Gris** — light and crisp (Italy) or fuller/spiced (Alsace)
- **Gewurztraminer** — highly aromatic, lychee, rose, low acidity

### Wine's structural components

| Component | Source | Sensation |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Alcohol | Yeast ferments sugar | Warmth, body |
| Acidity | Grapes + fermentation | Freshness, mouthwatering |
| Tannin | Skins, seeds, stems, oak | Drying/grippy sensation (reds) |
| Residual sugar | Unfermented grape sugar | Sweetness |
| Body | All of the above combined | Weight on palate (light / medium / full) |

## Implications

- Tannin content drives red wine food pairings: high-tannin wines cut through protein-rich dishes.
- Acidity makes wine feel lighter and more food-friendly regardless of colour.
- Fortification kills the yeast and stops fermentation, so fortified wines are almost always sweet unless fermentation was allowed to complete before fortifying (e.g. dry Sherries).
- Sparkling wines have higher apparent acidity than still wines due to dissolved CO₂ converting to carbonic acid.

## Misconceptions

- **"Rosé is just cheap blended red and white."** In most regions, rosé is made by brief skin contact only; blending is permitted mainly in Champagne.
- **"White wine doesn't have tannins."** It has trace tannins; white wines fermented on skins ("orange wine") have significant tannin.
- **"Dry = not fruity."** Fruit flavour and residual sugar are separate axes. A bone-dry Shiraz can taste very fruity.
- **"All sweet wines are dessert wines."** Off-dry Riesling or Vouvray pairs brilliantly with savoury food (spicy Asian, charcuterie).
