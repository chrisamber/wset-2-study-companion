# Wine Serving Temperature (WSET Level 1)

## Analogy

Temperature is the EQ on a wine. Too warm and you boost the low end — alcohol and oak become loud, fruit turns jammy, acidity hides. Too cold and you high-pass everything useful: aromas shut down, tannins go harsh, bubbles disappear. The right temperature is the flat, transparent setting where the wine speaks for itself.

## Mechanics

### Quick-reference table (WSET 1 standard)

| Wine category                                                   | Serving temperature | Practical guide                                          |
| --------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Sparkling (Champagne, Cava, Prosecco)                           | **6–10 °C**         | 2–3 hrs in fridge; 20–30 min in ice bucket               |
| Sweet / dessert (Sauternes, TBA Riesling)                       | **6–10 °C**         | Same as sparkling — cold restrains cloying sweetness     |
| Light-bodied, crisp whites (Pinot Grigio, Albariño, Muscadet)   | **7–10 °C**         | Bottom of fridge (~1–2 hrs)                              |
| Medium–full-bodied whites / oaked whites (Chardonnay, Viognier) | **10–13 °C**        | 45 min in fridge; or out of fridge 15 min before serving |
| Rosé                                                            | **7–10 °C**         | Same as crisp whites                                     |
| Light-bodied reds (Pinot Noir, Gamay / Beaujolais)              | **12–15 °C**        | 15–20 min in fridge before serving                       |
| Medium–full-bodied reds (Merlot, Syrah, Cabernet Sauvignon)     | **15–18 °C**        | Cellar/room temp in a cool home; no fridge               |
| Fortified — dry (Fino/Manzanilla Sherry)                        | **7–10 °C**         | Treat like a crisp white; serve from fridge              |
| Fortified — sweet/aged (Port, Oloroso, Madeira)                 | **14–18 °C**        | Slightly below room temperature                          |

### What goes wrong at the wrong temperature

**Too warm (+3–5 °C above ideal):**
- Alcohol becomes hot and pungent on the nose
- Fruit smells jammy/stewed
- Acidity seems lower (wine tastes flabby)
- Tannins integrate but wine can feel heavy
- Bubbles go flat faster (sparkling)

**Too cold (−3–5 °C below ideal):**
- Aromas shut down (vapour pressure drops)
- Tannins seem harsh and astringent
- Acidity becomes aggressive
- Sweetness is masked
- Bubbles feel sharp and harsh

### Practical rules of thumb

1. **When in doubt, serve 2 °C cooler than target** — wine warms quickly in the glass and from your hand; it's easier to let it warm than to chill a glass already poured.
2. **Ice bucket = fastest chilling** — equal parts ice and water, 15–20 min drops a room-temp bottle to serving range.
3. **"Room temperature" is a historical artefact** — medieval French cellars were 14–16 °C, not 22 °C modern living rooms. Most reds benefit from 10–15 min in the fridge before serving.
4. **Fino Sherry is the most common mistake** — it is a dry white wine functionally and should be served ice cold and consumed within days of opening.

## Implications

- Temperature is one of the simplest, highest-leverage improvements in wine service — more impactful than decanting for most everyday bottles.
- Light red grapes like Pinot Noir and Gamay have delicate aromatics that express best slightly below room temperature; they share more in common with full-bodied whites than with big reds in serving terms.
- Chilling suppresses sweetness perception: serving a sweet wine too warm makes it taste cloying; serving it cold balances it.

## Misconceptions

- **"Reds are always room temperature."** Only if your room is 15–18 °C. In most modern homes reds are served 3–5 °C too warm.
- **"You can't chill red wine."** Light reds (Beaujolais, Pinot Noir) actively benefit from 10–15 min in the fridge.
- **"Sparkling wine should be as cold as possible."** Below 6 °C, the carbonation feels harsh and aromas vanish.
- **"Port is served warm."** Vintage Port and Ruby Port are fine at 16–18 °C, but Tawny Port can be served slightly chilled (12–14 °C) in Portugal.
