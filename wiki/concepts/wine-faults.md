# Wine Faults

## Analogy

A faulty wine is like a carton of milk that's gone off. The milk is still milk — same colour, same carton — but one whiff tells you something's wrong, and no amount of stirring fixes it. Wine faults work the same way: they're unpleasant sensory characteristics (in taste, smell, or appearance) that arise from chemical or microbial problems and overwhelm the fruit you expected. Just as you trust your nose on the milk, most wine faults reveal themselves on the nose — the smell is your first and best alarm.

## Mechanics

Wine faults diminish quality through taste, smell, or appearance, arising from either chemical or microbial origins — caused by poor winemaking or inadequate storage. **The nose detects the vast majority of faults.** Here are the ones a L2 candidate must recognise.

### Cork taint (TCA) — "corked"

The most notorious fault, primarily caused by **2,4,6-trichloroanisole (TCA)**, which originates as a metabolite of mould growth on chlorine-bleached corks and barrels. TCA accounts for roughly **80–85% of cork taint cases** and affects about **1–5% of bottled wines** industry-wide.

- **Smell:** earthy, mouldy, musty — "wet cardboard," "damp newspaper," "wet dog." It masks and suppresses the wine's natural fruit.
- **Palate:** texture stays intact but the aromatic finish is stripped away; the wine smells and tastes "wrong."
- **No visual change.** Only **3–4 nanograms per litre** can ruin an entire bottle. The difficulty of spotting it at subtle levels has driven the move to screw caps.

### Oxidation

One of the most prevalent faults, needing only oxygen plus a catalyst. Oxygen attacks phenolic compounds (anthocyanins, catechins), causing colour loss, flavour loss, and flattened aromas. Excess oxygen can activate *Acetobacter*, converting ethanol back into **acetaldehyde** (which gives sherry-like character plus green apple, sour, and metallic notes).

- **Appearance:** whites deepen to yellow/amber; reds develop brick/brown colour.
- **Smell/palate:** sherry-like, bruised/green apple, flat, stale, sour.
- **Remedy/prevention:** winemakers add sulfur dioxide (SO₂) or erythorbic acid to protect the wine and bind oxidation byproducts.

### Volatile acidity (VA)

**Acetic acid** ("vinegar taint") comes from spoilage organisms, particularly *Acetobacter* and *Gluconobacter*. **Ethyl acetate**, formed from ethanol and acetic acid, smells of **vinegar, paint thinner, and nail polish remover**. On the palate VA creates burning, acidic sensations. Sensory threshold for acetic acid exceeds 700 mg/L; above ~1.2–1.3 g/L it is distinctly unpleasant.

### Reduction (sulfur compounds)

From sulfur compounds formed in winemaking, with very low sensory thresholds.

- **Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S):** forms during fermentation in nitrogen-poor conditions; smells of **rotten egg**.
- **Mercaptans (thiols):** form when finished wine sits too long on lees; smell of **onion, rubber, skunk**. Racking prevents this.

### Brettanomyces ("Brett")

The yeast *Brettanomyces* consumes residual sugars after primary fermentation and produces volatile phenols:

- **4-ethylphenol:** band-aids, barnyard, horse stable, antiseptic ("sweaty saddle").
- **4-ethylguaiacol:** bacon, spice, cloves, smoky.
- At low levels some producers feel it adds complexity; unchecked it turns "mousy" and masks the underlying variety. **Infected barrels cannot be salvaged and must be discarded.**

### Heat damage (maderisation) — "cooked"

From storage well above the ideal **13°C (55°F)**. Heat speeds ageing and can force wine past the cork (causing leakage). Reportedly the most widespread wine problem, but often goes unnoticed.

- **Appearance:** reds develop brick colour; cork may be displaced or leaking, with wine traces along or above the cork.
- **Smell/palate:** cooked/stewed, flat; the wine becomes oxidised.

### Light strike (*goût de lumière*)

Excess UV exposure (325–450 nm). Delicate wines like **Champagne** suffer most, developing **wet cardboard / wet wool** characteristics. Reds rarely succumb thanks to protective phenolics.

### Refermentation

Residual yeast ferments leftover sugar in the bottle, usually from non-sterile bottling. Causes **turbidity, slight carbonation, and coarse odours**. Bubbles in a still red wine signal this fault (though it occurs naturally in young Vinho Verde). Prevented by bottling dry, sterile filtering, or adding dimethyl dicarbonate.

### Fault → cause → sign

| Fault | Cause | Tell-tale sign |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| Cork taint (TCA) | TCA from mould on chlorine-bleached cork | Musty, wet cardboard / wet dog; fruit muted; no visual change |
| Oxidation | Oxygen exposure; *Acetobacter*; acetaldehyde | Browning (reds brick), amber whites; sherry/bruised-apple, flat, sour |
| Volatile acidity | Acetic acid / ethyl acetate from spoilage organisms | Vinegar, nail polish remover; burning, acidic palate |
| Reduction | H₂S (low nitrogen) / mercaptans (lees) | Rotten egg, rubber, onion, skunk |
| Brettanomyces | *Brettanomyces* yeast; 4-EP & 4-EG | Barnyard, horse stable, band-aids, clove, smoky; turns "mousy" |
| Heat damage | Storage above ~13°C; oxidation | Cooked/stewed, flat; reds go brick; cork leaking/displaced |
| Light strike | Excess UV (esp. Champagne) | Wet cardboard / wet wool |
| Refermentation | Residual yeast + sugar; non-sterile bottling | Turbidity, bubbles in still wine, coarse odours |

## Implications

- **Trust your nose first.** Most faults are aromatic — in service, smell the wine before pouring for a guest. Visual cues back this up: yellowing/browning signals oxidation; gas bubbles in a still wine signal refermentation; a burning, acidic sensation signals volatile acidity.
- **Recognising a corked bottle in service:** the fruit goes missing and the wine smells musty/like wet cardboard, with no colour change. This is grounds to replace the bottle.
- **Faults vs intentional style.** Some wines undergo oxidation *deliberately* — notably certain **Sherries** and French Vin Jaune — where sherry-like, oxidative character is desirable, not a fault. Likewise a touch of Brett is seen by some producers as added complexity. The line is intent and balance: when the character overwhelms the wine, it's a fault.

## Misconceptions

- **"Everything off must be cork taint."** Because TCA is so notorious, consumers wrongly blame it for other faults — oxidation, VA, heat damage and Brett are all separate.
- **"Tartrate crystals are a fault."** They're harmless salts (glass-like in whites, brownish-red in reds), easily removed by decanting — not a defect.
- **"Cloudiness means the wine is spoiled."** Haze from proteins eventually stabilises naturally; it's not inherently a fault.
- **"Oxidative, sherry-like notes are always a flaw."** In Fino/Oloroso Sherry and Vin Jaune that character is intentional and prized.
- **"Bubbles in any wine are bad."** A light spritz charms in young whites (and is natural in Vinho Verde); only in a mature still red do bubbles point to refermentation.
