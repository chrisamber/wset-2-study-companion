# Sherry

## Analogy

Think of Sherry like a sourdough starter that never gets thrown out. The barrels are arranged in a tiered stack (the *solera* system), and every time wine is drawn off the bottom for bottling, the gap is topped up from the tier above, which is topped from the tier above that. No barrel is ever fully emptied, so every glass contains a blend of many years — including traces of very old wine. Just as a baker keeps feeding the same starter for decades to hold a consistent flavour, the Sherry house keeps "feeding" its solera to hold a consistent house style across harvests.

The other half of Sherry's identity is sequence: it ferments **dry first, then gets fortified**. That single fact — spirit added *after* fermentation finishes — is the key contrast with [[port]] and explains why a base Sherry has no sugar left.

## Mechanics

### Where and from what

Sherry (*vino de Jerez*) is a fortified wine made from white grapes grown near **Jerez de la Frontera** in Andalusia, **Spain**. The dominant grape is **Palomino** (used for Fino, Manzanilla, Amontillado, Oloroso and most blended styles); **Pedro Ximénez (PX)** is the other key grape, used for the intensely sweet PX style and as a sweetening component.

### When and how it is fortified (the key exam fact)

The production sequence is: harvest → press and **ferment the must fully** → classify the base wines → **fortify**. Because fermentation runs to completion *before* grape spirit is added, **the base wine is dry — there is no residual sugar.** As the source puts it, "most sherries are initially dry, with any sweetness being added later."

This is the fundamental contrast with [[port]], which is fortified *during* fermentation: adding high-strength spirit mid-ferment kills the yeast and arrests fermentation, leaving residual sugar. Sherry's spirit goes in *after*, so any sweetness in a Sherry is **blended in afterwards**, never left over from a stopped ferment.

Crucially, the fortification level doesn't set sweetness — it sets the **ageing path**:
- **~15–15.5% ABV** → flor yeast survives → **biological ageing** → Fino / Manzanilla
- **~17%+ ABV** → flor cannot survive → **oxidative ageing** → Oloroso

### Two ageing paths

**Biological ageing (under flor).** Fino and Manzanilla age beneath *velo de flor* — a living veil of indigenous yeast. The lower fortification (~15.5%) lets flor survive. The flor shields the wine from oxygen while metabolically transforming it: it consumes alcohol, reduces glycerine and volatile acidity, and generates acetaldehydes that give sharp, fresh, yeasty aromatic notes. Result: pale, delicate, dry wines.

**Oxidative ageing.** With no flor (alcohol too high), the wine ages in direct contact with oxygen seeping through the porous oak butt walls, darkening and concentrating as water transpires through the wood (*merma*: 3–5% annual evaporative loss). Result: darker, richer wines with nutty, walnut and dried-fruit aromas. **Amontillado** sits in between — it starts under flor (biological), then the flor dies and it finishes oxidatively, combining yeasty and nutty character.

**Manzanilla** is simply a Fino made in the coastal town of **Sanlúcar de Barrameda**, where the humid sea-side micro-climate grows a thicker, more active flor, giving a distinctive saline, marine character.

### The solera system

Wines age in the *criaderas y solera* system — tiers of barrels by age. The **solera** is the bottom tier (oldest wine, ready for bottling); the **criaderas** above hold progressively younger wine. In *correr escalas* ("running the scales"), wine is drawn (*saca*) from the solera for bottling and the gap is refilled (*rocío*) from the first criadera, which is topped from the second, and so on upward; the youngest wine enters at the top. No barrel is fully emptied, so every bottling is a blend of many vintages. Sherry requires a **minimum of two years** of solera ageing. Premium age marks: **VOS** (average age ≥ 20 years), **VORS** (≥ 30 years).

### Styles at a glance

| Style | Grape | Ageing | Flor? | ABV | Sweetness | Character |
|-------|-------|--------|-------|-----|-----------|-----------|
| Fino | Palomino | Biological | Yes | ~15% | Dry (<4 g/L) | Pale straw; almond, fresh dough, herbs; light and delicate |
| Manzanilla | Palomino | Biological (Sanlúcar) | Yes (thicker) | ~15% | Dry (<4 g/L) | Fino character + saline, marine notes |
| Amontillado | Palomino | Bio → oxidative | Initially yes | 17–20% | Dry (<5 g/L) | Amber; almonds turning to walnut/hazelnut |
| Oloroso | Palomino | Oxidative | No | ≥17% | Dry (<5 g/L) | Dark amber/mahogany; walnut, dried fruit, nutty |
| Pale Cream | Palomino | Biological + sweetened | Yes (base) | ~15.5% | 45–115 g/L | Sweetened Fino base; stays pale |
| Medium | Palomino | Typically oxidative + sweetened | No | 15–22% | 5–115 g/L | Amber; moderately sweet, nutty |
| Cream | Palomino | Oxidative + sweetened (PX) | No | 15–22% | 115–140 g/L | Dark amber; sweet, rich, nutty, raisined |
| Pedro Ximénez | Pedro Ximénez | Oxidative | No | 15–22% | >212 g/L | Ebony-black, viscous; raisins, figs, dates, chocolate |

**On sweetening:** Pale Cream, Medium, Cream and PX all get their sugar *added by blending* after the dry base is made. **PX** is made from sun-dried (*asoleo*) raisined grapes, exceeding 212 g/L sugar — among the sweetest wines in the world — and is the primary sweetening wine (e.g. PX added to a dry Oloroso base makes Cream Sherry).

## Implications

- **Fino and Manzanilla behave like dry white wines.** Serve them **chilled at 6–8 °C** as an aperitif, and consume fresh — see [[wine-serving-temperature]]. Classic Fino pairings: tapas, olives, nuts, *jamón*, shellfish, anchovies, cold soups (gazpacho, *ajo blanco*).
- **The exam loves the fortification-timing contrast.** Lock it in: **Sherry is fortified AFTER fermentation (base is dry); [[port]] is fortified DURING fermentation (sugar remains).** If asked which leaves residual sugar from a stopped ferment, that's Port, not Sherry.
- **Fortification level → ageing path → style.** Remember the lever: low alcohol keeps flor alive (Fino/Manzanilla); high alcohol kills it (Oloroso); Amontillado crosses from one to the other.
- **Any sweet Sherry was sweetened deliberately** by blending — usually with PX — never by interrupting fermentation.

## Misconceptions

- **"All Sherry is sweet."** No — the base wine is always fermented dry; Fino, Manzanilla, Amontillado and Oloroso are dry styles, and sweetness only appears in deliberately blended styles.
- **"Sherry and Port are fortified the same way."** No — Sherry is fortified *after* fermentation completes; Port is fortified *during* fermentation to leave residual sugar.
- **"Fino and Oloroso differ because of the grape."** No — both are Palomino; they differ because of fortification level and therefore ageing path (flor vs oxidative).
- **"Flor is just decoration."** No — it is a living yeast veil that shields the wine from oxygen and actively reshapes it (consuming alcohol, cutting volatile acidity, creating fresh acetaldehyde aromas).
- **"Manzanilla is a different grape or wine from Fino."** No — it is a Fino made in Sanlúcar de Barrameda, where a thicker flor gives it a saline, marine edge.
