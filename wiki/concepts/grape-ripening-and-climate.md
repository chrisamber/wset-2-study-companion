# Grape Ripening and Climate

## Analogy

Think of green tomatoes ripening on a sunny windowsill. Hard, sour and green at first, they soften, sweeten, change colour, and turn fragrant as they sit in the warmth. A grape does the same on the vine: as it ripens, sugar climbs, acid drops, colour and tannin develop, and the aroma shifts from "green" to "fruity." How much sun and warmth the windowsill (the climate) gets decides how far that shift travels — and that, more than anything, decides the style of the finished wine.

## Mechanics

### The growing season in order

The vine's year runs bud break → flowering → fruit set → veraison → harvest, then leaf fall and dormancy.

- **Flowering** (40–80 days after bud break) needs average daily temperatures of 15–20 °C. Cold, wind or rain can prevent fertilisation.
- **Fruit set** follows almost immediately and fixes the potential crop size. Berries here are green, hard, low in sugar and very high in acid. (*Coulure* = berries fail to set or drop; *millerandage* = some berries stay small and seedless.)
- **Veraison** ("change of colour," ~40–50 days after fruit set) is the official start of ripening — berries soften, change colour, start accumulating sugar and begin losing acid.

### What changes as a grape ripens

- **Sugar rises.** Sucrose from photosynthesis converts to glucose and fructose. More sugar means more potential alcohol (measured in degrees Brix: ~21.7 °Bx ≈ 12% alcohol, ~27.1 °Bx ≈ 15%).
- **Acid falls.** Acid drops through dilution as the berry grows and through respiration. The two main acids are tartaric and malic; **malic degrades faster, especially in warmth.** High-acid wines come from cooler climates and/or earlier-picked grapes.
- **Tannins ripen (black grapes).** Tannins live in skins, seeds and stems. Early they are bitter and "green"; with ripening they soften and polymerise. Green tannins = under-ripe.
- **Colour develops (black grapes).** Anthocyanins replace chlorophyll, turning berries from green to red/black through and after veraison.
- **Aromas shift.** Herbaceous compounds (methoxypyrazines — the green, bell-pepper note) degrade and fruity aromas build. Whites move green apple/cut grass → citrus → stone → tropical; blacks move green-pepper/stalky → red cherry/raspberry/plum → blackcurrant jam/prune/dried fruit.

### Ripeness stages

- **Unripe:** hard, green, low sugar, very high (mostly malic) acid, herbaceous flavours.
- **Ripe:** sugars up, acids down, fruit aromas developed, balance suited to dry table wine.
- **Over-ripe / extra-ripe:** left too long — very high sugar, low acid, dehydrated; used for late-harvest and dessert styles.

### Climate, weather and geography

The vine starts growing around 10 °C; flowering wants ~17–20 °C; **optimal ripening is around 27 °C.** Most premium wine grows between the **30th and 50th parallels**, north or south. Climate types in the source:

- **Mediterranean:** long, moderate-to-warm season, little rain, warm winters → ripe, full-bodied, lower-acid wines.
- **Continental:** marked seasons, hot summers, cold winters, wide **diurnal** (day-to-night) swings that preserve acidity; big vintage variation.
- **Maritime:** moderated by water, warm (not hot) summers, long season; rain/humidity can bring disease.

Geographic and topographic levers on ripeness:
- **Latitude:** lower (nearer equator) = warmer, more sun; higher = cooler.
- **Altitude:** ~0.6 °C cooler per 100 m gained; longer ripening, bigger diurnal swing → ripeness *and* retained acidity.
- **Slope / aspect:** sun-facing slopes (south in N hemisphere, north in S) are warmer; slopes also drain better.
- **Soils:** affect drainage, heat and vigour; well-drained soils concentrate flavour.
- **Seas / rivers:** moderate temperature, reduce frost, extend the season; rivers also reflect light and improve airflow.
- **Fog / cloud:** cool mornings and reduced sun slow ripening and retain acidity.

Hazards: drought (vine stress, uneven ripening), spring frost (destroys shoots/yield), hail (damages berries), and excessive rain near harvest (dilutes juice, triggers grey rot).

### Cool vs moderate vs warm — the master table

| Feature | Cool climate | Moderate climate | Warm climate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sugar accumulation | Lower | Medium | Higher |
| Acidity | High (malic preserved) | Medium | Low |
| Alcohol / body | Low / light | Medium | High / full |
| White fruit character | Citrus, green apple, herbaceous | Stone fruit, riper | Tropical, jammy |
| Black fruit character | Red berry, lighter colour, firmer tannin grip | Riper red berry | Dark fruit, jammy/dried, softer tannins |
| Typical signature | Bright, tart, mineral, lithe | Balanced | Rich, ripe, silky |

Diurnal shifts matter at every climate: cool nights preserve malic acid, giving freshness and a mineral profile even where summer days are warm.

### Concentrating sugar for sweet wines

- **Late harvest:** grapes left on the vine accumulate sugar by continued photosynthesis and dehydration → concentrated, lower-acid sweet wine.
- **Noble rot (*Botrytis cinerea*):** thrives ~20 °C and above 80% humidity, needing ripe grapes then alternating misty mornings / dry afternoons. The fungus shrivels berries (mass can fall up to 60%), consumes tartaric acid, and builds peach/apricot/honey aromas — high acid keeps the sweetness from cloying. Examples: Sauternes (Sémillon), Tokaji Aszú (Furmint), Trockenbeerenauslese (Riesling).
- **Frozen grapes (Icewine):** pressed while frozen; ice (water) is removed, leaving very sweet, high-acid juice.

## Implications

- **One framework predicts style.** Knowing where a grape was grown — cool, moderate or warm — lets you predict acidity, alcohol, body and fruit profile. This is the backbone of blind tasting: high acid + low alcohol + citrus/green = cool; full body + low acid + jammy/dark fruit = warm.
- **Same grape, two faces.** [[chardonnay]] in a cool zone is lean and citrussy; in a warm zone it is full and tropical. [[riesling]] keeps its high acid in cool climates — its calling card. [[syrah-shiraz]] reads peppery and red-fruited when cool, dark and jammy when warm.
- **Diurnal range is the cheat code.** [[pinot-noir]] and other delicate grapes can be grown in warmer or high-altitude spots and still keep freshness if nights are cold.
- **Sweetness has three routes** (late harvest, noble rot, freezing) — distinguish them by *how* the sugar got concentrated, which shapes acidity and aroma.

## Misconceptions

- **"Warmer is always better."** No — too much heat drops acidity and can make jammy, flabby, over-alcoholic wine; balance, not maximum ripeness, is the goal.
- **"Veraison means the grape is ripe."** Veraison only *starts* ripening — 30–70 more days follow before harvest.
- **"All grape acid is the same."** Tartaric and malic differ; malic burns off faster in warmth, which is why cool climates keep their freshness.
- **"Noble rot and grey rot are the same."** Noble rot (*Botrytis* on ripe grapes with the right humidity cycle) concentrates and enriches; grey rot from harvest rain ruins the crop.
- **"High latitude just means cooler, full stop."** Altitude, aspect, water bodies, fog and diurnal swing can override raw latitude — that's why cool-tasting wines come from sunny places.
