# Winemaking Process (WSET L2)

## Analogy

Making wine is like brewing a cup of tea where the grape is both the tea leaf and the sugar. Yeast is the "kettle" that does the work — it eats the grape's sugar and turns it into alcohol plus fizz (CO₂). How long you leave the dark skins steeping decides the colour and grip, just as a longer steep gives stronger, more tannic tea. And whether you brew in a fresh wooden cup (new oak) or a clean glass one (stainless steel) changes the flavour: the wood adds vanilla and spice, the glass adds nothing and keeps the fruit pure.

## Mechanics

### The core transformation: alcoholic fermentation

Yeast metabolises the sugars in grape juice into **ethanol (alcohol) + carbon dioxide (CO₂)**. Primary fermentation runs roughly **5–14 days** (about one to two weeks). Roughly **every gram of sugar converts to about half a gram of alcohol** — so a must needs ~24% sugar to reach ~12% alcohol.

Yeast can be **wild/ambient** (regional character) or **cultured** (e.g. *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*, more predictable); different strains affect the vigour of fermentation and enhance or suppress certain flavours.

### The five stages

Harvesting → **Crushing** (gently breaks skins to release contents) → **Fermentation** → **Pressing** (force separates juice from skins/solids) → **Maturation** → **Blending** → Packaging.

- **Free-run juice** flows out immediately after crushing and is typically higher quality.
- **Pressed juice** yields 15–30% more volume per ton but carries more phenolics/tannins (herbaceous notes); presses ramp ~0–2.0 Bar and allow "press cuts".

### Red vs white vs rosé

| | Skin contact | How it's made | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Red** | Yes — throughout fermentation | Ferment the must (pulp + juice) **with the skins**; maceration extracts colour, flavour, tannin | Colour and tannin |
| **White** | None | Press crushed grapes, **remove skins**, ferment the juice alone | No skin colour/tannin |
| **Rosé** | Brief | Crush, leave dark skins in contact **just long enough** for desired colour, then press and ferment as a white (short maceration) | Light colour, little tannin |

- **Rosé by blending** (a little red into white) is *less common* for still wines.
- **Orange wine** = maceration in the manner of rosé/red, but using **white grape varieties**.

### Fermentation temperature

| Style | Temperature | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **White (cool)** | ~12–18 °C (also cited 15–18 / 18–20 °C) | Preserves delicate aromas, fresh-fruit/floral character, esters; slower, lower off-flavour risk |
| **Red (warm)** | ~20–30 °C (also cited 22–25 °C) | Greater colour/tannin/phenolic extraction, more body; can lose delicate aromatics |

Winemakers sometimes ferment reds cool "to bring out more fruit flavours." Excessive heat can stall fermentation or drive off volatile compounds.

### Malolactic fermentation (MLF)

A **bacterial** conversion (after primary fermentation): tart **malic acid → softer lactic acid**, releasing CO₂. It lowers titratable acidity by 1–3 g/L and raises pH ~0.3.

- Gives a **rounder, fuller mouthfeel** and softer acidity.
- Byproduct **diacetyl** = "buttery" aroma, especially prized in Chardonnay.
- **Most red wines** undergo full MLF (reduces acidity, prevents bottle fermentation); ~20% of whites do — Chardonnay commonly, but it is **deliberately prevented in Riesling and Gewürztraminer** to keep them tart and aromatic.

### Lees and bâtonnage

Keeping wine on the yeast sediment (**sur lie**) enhances body and flavour persistence and adds palate softness. **Stirring the lees (bâtonnage)** increases contact, adding creaminess and savoury complexity.

### Oak vs neutral vessels

- **New, small oak** imparts the most flavour and oxygen; common barrels are Bordeaux 225 L and Burgundy 228 L. Larger formats (hogsheads 300 L, foudres) have a lower surface-to-volume ratio → gentler, subtler.
- After **3–5 harvests** old oak imparts little or no flavour; oxygen permeability is severely restricted after ~5 years.
- **Toast level**: lighter toasting keeps more tannin/wood flavour; heavy charring adds complexity but reduces some aromatics.
- Oak flavours: **vanilla, caramel, smoke, toast, spice** (Chardonnay in oak → coconut, cinnamon, cloves); contributes ellagitannins and vanillin, softens texture, and allows slow micro-oxygenation that softens red tannins and stabilises colour.
- **Stainless steel** (most common neutral vessel): preserves primary fruit, precise temperature control. **Concrete** (incl. egg shape): slight oxygen exchange, no wood flavour. **Oak chips/staves/cubes** add oak flavour cheaply in steel (associated with everyday wine).

### Sweetness, adjustments, and post-ferment

- **Stopping fermentation early** (leaves residual sugar): chill the must to stun the yeast, add SO₂, sterile-filter out yeast/bacteria, or add high alcohol/brandy to kill yeast. Late harvest, freezing grapes, or *Botrytis cinerea* concentrate sugar; high sugar + rising alcohol can stop fermentation naturally.
- **Fortification** = adding grape spirit (brandy) to kill yeast (used for Port and other fortified styles).
- **Chaptalisation** = adding sugar to boost alcohol (restricted in some regions, e.g. California). **Acidification** (add tartaric/citric acid) freshens warm-climate wine; **de-acidification** softens cool-climate must.
- **Blending** corrects inadequacies, adjusts acid/tannin, builds house style and vintage consistency. **Fining** (gelatin, egg white, isinglass, bentonite, diatomaceous earth) removes tannins/astringency and clarifies.
- **Bottling**: final sulphite for preservation, then cork, synthetic closure, or screw cap.

## Implications

- **Tannin and colour in your glass come from skin contact** — so reds have grip and rosés barely any. If a white tastes tannic/grippy, it's usually oak, not skins.
- **Oak shows up as vanilla, spice, toast and a creamier body**; if a wine is pure, fresh and fruity with none of that, it was likely made in stainless steel.
- **A buttery, soft, low-acid white** has been through MLF (think oaked Chardonnay); a zingy, tart, aromatic white (Riesling, Gewürztraminer) had MLF blocked.
- **Cool fermentation preserves freshness and aroma; warm fermentation extracts more body and structure** — the same grape can be steered toward either style.
- **Residual sweetness means fermentation was stopped early**, not that sugar was added afterwards.

## Misconceptions

- **"Rosé is red wine blended with white."** Only sometimes, and even then mainly for *sparkling*; still rosé is normally short skin contact (bleed/maceration), not blending.
- **"Red and white grapes give red and white wine."** The colour comes from **skin contact**, not the grape — white wine is the pressed juice with skins removed; orange wine is white grapes macerated like a red.
- **"Malolactic fermentation is a kind of yeast fermentation."** It is a **bacterial** conversion of malic to lactic acid, separate from yeast-driven alcoholic fermentation.
- **"Oak barrels last forever."** After 3–5 harvests an old barrel imparts little flavour and its useful oxygen exchange is largely gone.
- **"Sweet wine has sugar added at the end."** Sweetness usually comes from **stopping fermentation early** so unfermented sugar remains; adding sugar (chaptalisation) is to raise *alcohol*, not residual sweetness.
