# Port

## Analogy

Think of fermentation as yeast eating sugar and exhaling alcohol. With Port, the winemaker walks in *mid-meal* and adds a shot of strong grape spirit (*aguardente*) — that jolt of high alcohol stuns the yeast and stops it dead before it has finished eating. The result: the leftover sugar stays in the glass (sweet) and the alcohol is already high (~19–22%). Compare this with [[sherry]], where the winemaker lets the yeast finish the whole meal *first* (so the base wine is dry) and only fortifies afterwards. The single fact that unlocks half the exam questions is *when* the spirit goes in: Port = **during** fermentation → sweet; Sherry = **after** → starts dry.

## Mechanics

### Where it comes from
Port is a Portuguese fortified wine produced in the **Douro Valley** of northern Portugal — schist-and-granite country sheltered from the Atlantic by the Serra do Marão mountains. The demarcated region has three sub-zones: **Baixo Corgo** (westernmost, wettest, makes inexpensive ruby/tawny), **Cima Corgo** (higher, better grapes for vintage, reserve and aged tawny), and **Douro Superior** (driest, warmest). Made from Portuguese grape varieties (the source does not name the individual red grapes; White Port is made from white Portuguese grapes).

### When and how it is fortified — the key exam fact
A neutral grape spirit (*aguardente*) is added **mid-fermentation**, *before* all the sugar has converted to alcohol. This does two things at once:
1. **Stops the yeast early** → unconverted grape sugar remains → the wine is **sweet**.
2. **Raises alcohol** to typically **19–22% ABV**.

This is the defining contrast with [[sherry]]: Sherry is fortified **after** fermentation finishes, so its base wine is dry. Port fortifies *during*, so it is sweet by design.

### Styles → character
Two families: **Ruby** styles (reductive — sealed tanks/bottle, fresh fruit) and **Tawny** styles (oxidative — wooden barrels, nutty/dried-fruit). Plus White Port.

| Style | Ageing | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Ruby (Fine Ruby)** | Concrete/stainless tanks, sealed (reductive) | Dark ruby colour, fresh fruity red/dark berry; ~100+ g/L residual sugar |
| **Reserve Ruby** | ~5+ years in barrel; IVDP-approved | Richer, fruitier than basic Ruby, softened alcohol; after-dinner drink |
| **Late Bottled Vintage (LBV)** | Single declared vintage; bottled 4–6 yrs after harvest | Dark fruit (cherry, plum, blackberry), pepper/spice; lighter tannins than Vintage; filtered (drink now) or unfiltered (decant) |
| **Vintage** | Single declared vintage; ≤~2.5 yrs barrel, then 10–40+ yrs bottle; unfiltered | Most prestigious; rich, full-bodied, structured tannins, concentrated fruit; throws sediment, decant |
| **Crusted** | Blend of vintages, bottled unfiltered with driven cork | Develops a "crust" of sediment in the bottle |
| **Rosé** | Like a rosé, limited skin contact (first released 2008) | Recent pink variant |
| **Basic Tawny** | Min. 3 yrs in barrel (oxidative) | Golden-brown, nutty |
| **Reserve Tawny** | ~6–7 yrs in barrel | Nutty, toffee/caramel hues |
| **Age-Indicated Tawny (10/20/30/40 Year)** | Blend matched to an average age | Increasingly nutty, dried-fruit, *rancio*; paler/more amber with age; most complex tawnies |
| **Colheita** | Single-vintage Tawny, min. 7 yrs in barrel | Shows the vintage year; extended oxidative ageing |
| **White Port** | — | White Portuguese grapes; dry / semi-dry / sweet (sweetest = *lagrima*); light body |

### Cask vs bottle: the core ageing distinction
- **Ruby family (reductive):** sealed tanks/bottles, minimal oxygen → keeps deep ruby colour and fresh fruit. Bottle development matters mostly for Vintage; basic styles change little.
- **Tawny family (oxidative):** small wooden barrels let oxygen in → colour fades to amber/tawny, develops nutty/dried-fruit/caramel flavours; loses volume to evaporation. Little change once bottled.
- Vintage Port is bottled *young* (short cask time) and does its developing **in bottle** over decades; Tawny does its developing **in cask**.

## Implications

- **Ruby and Vintage = fruity, bottle-aged.** If a tasting note says fresh red/dark berry and deep ruby colour, think Ruby family; Vintage is the age-worthy, decant-it peak of that family.
- **Tawny = nutty/oxidative, cask-aged.** Amber colour + walnut/caramel/dried-fruit = Tawny; the higher the age indication (10/20/30/40), the more *rancio* complexity and the paler the colour.
- **Fortification timing is the most testable fact:** Port fortified *during* fermentation → sweet; [[sherry]] fortified *after* → starts dry. Expect a question that hinges on this contrast.
- **Service:** Vintage and Crusted are unfiltered and **need decanting** (sediment). White Port is often served **chilled** as an aperitif or as a "Port Tonic." For serving temperatures of sweet/aged Ports, see [[wine-serving-temperature]].
- **Single Quinta Vintage** comes from one estate, typically in years a multi-quinta vintage is *not* declared — a useful distractor to recognise.

## Misconceptions

- **"All Port is fortified after fermentation, like Sherry."** No — Port is fortified *during* fermentation, which is exactly why it stays sweet; Sherry fortifies after, so it starts dry.
- **"Tawny is just old Ruby that faded with age in the bottle."** No — Tawny's colour and nutty character come from *oxidative ageing in wooden barrels*, not from bottle age.
- **"Vintage Port spends decades in barrel."** No — it gets only ~2–2.5 years in cask, then develops for 10–40+ years *in bottle*.
- **"LBV and Vintage are the same."** No — LBV is bottled 4–6 years after harvest with lighter tannins and an approachable style; Vintage is more structured, ages far longer, and must be decanted.
- **"White Port is always sweet."** No — it ranges dry through semi-dry to sweet (the sweetest labelled *lagrima*).
