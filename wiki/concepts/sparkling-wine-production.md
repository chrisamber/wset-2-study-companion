# Sparkling Wine Production

## Analogy

Think of a fizzy drink that makes its own bubbles in a sealed container. Take a finished still wine, add a little yeast and sugar, and seal it up. The yeast eats the sugar and gives off carbon dioxide (CO₂) — exactly as in any fermentation — but because the container is closed, the gas has nowhere to escape, so it dissolves into the wine. Open the bottle later and the trapped CO₂ rushes out as bubbles. Every L2 method is a variation on this one trick: a **second fermentation that traps CO₂**. The only real question is *where* you trap it — in the bottle or in a tank.

## Mechanics

### How the bubbles get there

After a normal first fermentation produces a dry base wine, the producer adds yeast and a small dose of sugar and seals the vessel. The yeast ferments that sugar; the CO₂ it produces cannot escape the closed system and dissolves into the wine. In a traditional-method bottle, pressure reaches about **6 bars**.

### Traditional Method (Méthode Champenoise / Méthode Traditionnelle)

The second fermentation happens **in the individual bottle**. The base wine is bottled with yeast and sugar (the *liqueur de tirage*) and sealed. Key stages:

- **Lees ageing / autolysis** — the wine ages on its spent yeast cells (lees). Extended contact produces the characteristic **bready, biscuity, yeasty** notes (with baked apple and caramel over long ageing) associated with Champagne.
- **Riddling (remuage)** — bottles sit on racks (pupitres) at a 35° angle and are rotated daily so sediment collects in the neck (now often done by mechanical gyropalettes).
- **Disgorgement (dégorgement)** — the neck is frozen and the plug of frozen sediment is expelled by the pressure.
- **Dosage** — a top-up of sugar dissolved in wine (*liqueur d'expédition*) sets the final sweetness, from bone-dry to sweet.

It is labour-intensive and costly, so it is used for premium wines. Bottle fermentation originated in Limoux (1531) before becoming linked with Champagne.

### Tank Method (Charmat / Metodo Martinotti)

The second fermentation happens **in a pressurised stainless steel tank**, not the bottle. Base wine, sugar and yeast go into a closed tank; the CO₂ cannot escape and dissolves into the wine. The yeast is then filtered out and the wine bottled under pressure. Because there is little or no lees ageing, the method **preserves the delicate primary fruit and floral aromas** of aromatic grapes — ideal for Prosecco and Asti. It is faster and considerably cheaper than the traditional method. (Patented by Federico Martinotti in 1895, improved by Eugène Charmat in 1907.)

### Method comparison

| Feature | Traditional Method | Tank / Charmat Method |
|---------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Where 2nd fermentation happens | In individual bottle | In pressurised steel tank |
| Lees contact / autolysis | Yes — bready, biscuity, yeasty | Minimal — fresh primary fruit/floral |
| Bubble character | Fine, persistent | Can be coarser |
| Cost | High (labour-intensive) | Lower (faster, scalable) |
| Key examples | Champagne, Cava, Crémant, Franciacorta DOCG, Méthode Cap Classique | Prosecco, Asti |
| Key grapes | Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, Meunier (Champagne); Macabeo, Xarel·lo, Parellada (Cava) | Glera (Prosecco); Moscato Bianco (Asti) |

### Key wines and their grapes

- **Champagne (France)** — traditional method; **Pinot Noir** (backbone), **Pinot Meunier** (fruit), **Chardonnay** (acidity, biscuit). *Blanc de Blancs* = 100% Chardonnay; *Blanc de Noirs* = dark-skinned grapes only.
- **Cava (Spain)** — traditional method (only traditional-method wines may be labelled Cava); ~95% from Penedès, Catalonia; Macabeo (Viura), Parellada, Xarel·lo.
- **Prosecco (Italy)** — tank method; **Glera** grape; fresh, crisp, fruit-forward (yellow apple, pear, peach, apricot). Tiers: Prosecco DOC and Prosecco Superiore DOCG.
- **Asti (Italy)** — tank method; **Moscato Bianco** in Piedmont; intensely floral, peach and apricot; traditionally sweet. (Moscato d'Asti is the lighter, lower-alcohol *frizzante* version.)

### Sweetness terms (driest → sweetest)

| Term | Residual sugar |
|------|----------------|
| Brut Nature / Zero Dosage | under 3 g/L |
| Extra Brut | under 6 g/L |
| Brut | under 12 g/L (most common today) |
| Extra Dry / Extra Sec | 12–17 g/L (confusingly off-dry) |
| Sec / Dry | 17–32 g/L |
| Demi-Sec | 32–50 g/L (noticeably sweet) |
| Doux | above 50 g/L (very sweet) |

### Vintage vs Non-Vintage (Champagne)

- **Non-Vintage (NV)** — a blend of multiple years for a consistent house style; minimum **15 months** on the lees.
- **Vintage** — entirely from one declared year (only exceptional years); minimum **3 years** on the lees.

## Implications

- **Method predicts style.** Traditional method → autolytic, biscuity/bready complexity (Champagne, Cava). Tank method → fresh, fruity, floral simplicity (Prosecco, Asti). If a tasting note says "brioche/biscuit," think traditional method; "fresh apple/pear/peach," think tank method.
- **Match wine to method on sight.** Champagne, Cava, Crémant, Franciacorta and Méthode Cap Classique = traditional method. Prosecco and Asti = tank method.
- **Brut is the default dry style** you will pour most often; **Extra Dry is sweeter than Brut** despite the name — a classic exam trap.
- **Watch the labelling/ageing facts** for Champagne: NV = 15 months minimum, Vintage = 3 years minimum on lees.

## Misconceptions

- **"All sparkling wine is made like Champagne."** No — Prosecco and Asti use the tank (Charmat) method, with the second fermentation in a tank, not the bottle.
- **"Extra Dry is the driest style."** No — Brut (under 12 g/L) is drier than Extra Dry (12–17 g/L); Brut Nature is the driest of all.
- **"Prosecco is just cheap Champagne."** No — different grape (Glera vs Champagne's trio) and different method (tank vs bottle), giving fresh fruit rather than biscuity autolysis.
- **"Cava is the Spanish tank-method fizz."** No — Cava must be made by the traditional method (same as Champagne); only traditional-method wines may be labelled Cava.
- **"The bubbles are pumped in."** In these L2 methods the CO₂ comes naturally from a trapped second fermentation, not from injected gas.
