# Wine Labelling and Geographical Indications

## Analogy

A wine label hierarchy is a **postal address read backwards, with a quality guarantee stapled on.** "Vin de France" is just "France" — country only, no street. "Bourgogne" is the city. A village name like "Gevrey-Chambertin" is the street. A Premier or Grand Cru vineyard is the exact house number. The tighter you zoom in, the more the rules say exactly what can be grown there and how — so a precise address is also a promise. That is the heart of the **place-led (Old World)** label: it tells you *where*, and from where you infer *what* and *how good*. The New World flips it — it prints the grape on the front like a brand name and lets the producer's reputation do the guaranteeing.

## Mechanics

### The EU framework: PDO vs PGI

Wine sits under a separate EU regulatory framework from other foods. The two protective schemes a candidate must know:

| Scheme | What it guarantees | Raw material | Production stages |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PDO** (Protected Designation of Origin) | Strongest link between wine and place | **All** raw material from the defined region | **Every** stage of production within the region |
| **PGI** (Protected Geographical Indication) | Quality/reputation attributable to the origin — looser | Not all must come from the area | **At least one** stage within the region |

The core distinction: how much raw material must come from the area, and how much of the production chain must occur within it. National quality designations (France's AOC, Italy's DOC/DOCG, Spain's DOCa/DO) run **in parallel** with the EU PDO/PGI labels. Under the dual-label system, French AOC wines may still show the traditional "AOC" mention alongside the EU "AOP" — both are legally equivalent.

### France — AOC/AOP

Administered by **INAO**; built on *terroir* (origin + traditional methods + local environmental and human factors).

| Tier | EU equivalent | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| **Vin de France** | — | Basic table wine, no geographical requirement |
| **IGP / Vin de Pays** | PGI | Broader region, stated origin; wider grape range, higher yields, broader practices. Label may show vintage and grape variety. ("Vin de Pays" was the pre-2009 name.) |
| **AOC / AOP** | PDO | Top tier; strictest on delimitation, permitted varieties, max yields, min natural alcohol, vinification |

**Within AOC** there are further internal levels — *Village* wines (e.g. Gevrey-Chambertin) outrank regional AOC, and *Premier Cru* / *Grand Cru* sit above (especially Burgundy and Alsace).

**Burgundy** (four levels by vineyard site / *climat*): Regional AOC (Bourgogne, ~52%) → Village/Communale (42 named villages, ~36%) → **Premier Cru** (specific vineyard, label shows village + climat + "1er Cru", ~12%, ~640 vineyards) → **Grand Cru** (33 vineyards, all Côte d'Or + Chablis Grand Cru; label shows **only the vineyard name** + "Grand Cru", village omitted, ~2%).

**Alsace Grand Cru**: 51 designated vineyards; wine must be one of four noble varieties (Riesling, Gewurztraminer, Pinot Gris, Muscat).

**Bordeaux 1855 Classification (Grand Cru Classé):** brokers ranked Médoc estates into five *growths*. **First Growths:** Lafite Rothschild, Margaux, Haut-Brion, Latour, and Mouton Rothschild (promoted 1973 — the only change since 1855). Sweet Sauternes/Barsac were ranked separately, with Château d'Yquem alone as "Premier Cru Supérieur." **Cru Bourgeois** = quality Médoc châteaux *not* in the 1855 list; relaunched 2010 as an annual quality mark, with three tiers from 2020 (Cru Bourgeois, Supérieur, Exceptionnel).

### Italy — four tiers

| Tier | EU equivalent | Key facts |
|---|---|---|
| **Vino da Tavola** (table wine) | — | Baseline; grape variety and vintage not required |
| **IGT** (Indicazione Geografica Tipica) | PGI | Broad area; grape/year/area optional on label. Introduced 1992; lets quality wines outside DOC rules (incl. international varieties) carry a GI — some "Super Tuscans" are IGT |
| **DOC** (Denominazione di Origine Controllata) | PDO | Precise area; mandatory chemical and taste (organoleptic) analysis; must have been IGT for ≥5 years |
| **DOCG** (...e Garantita) | PDO | Premium; stricter than DOC; numbered control bands (*fascette*) on the neck; batch analysis; must first have been DOC |

**Italian label terms:** **Classico** = the historic/central zone of an appellation (e.g. Chianti Classico), not a separate tier. **Riserva** = extended ageing beyond the minimum (e.g. Chianti Classico Riserva ≥24 months; Barolo Riserva 62 months). **Superiore** = higher min alcohol and/or longer ageing.

### Spain — DOCa/DO and ageing terms

| Tier | EU equivalent | Key facts |
|---|---|---|
| **Vino de Mesa (VdM)** | — | Table wine, no GI |
| **Vino de la Tierra (VT)** | PGI | Broad area, lower thresholds than DO (≈ French IGP) |
| **VC** (Vino de Calidad con IG) | PGI | Intermediate category (2003), above IGP but below DO |
| **DO** (Denominación de Origen) | PDO | Mainstream quality appellation; each has a *consejo regulador*; 60+ DOs |
| **DOCa** (Denom. de Origen Calificada) | PDO | Highest tier; only **two** regions: **Rioja** (1991) and **Priorat** (2003) |
| **VP** (Vino de Pago) | — | Single-estate wines of high reputation (2003) |

**Spanish ageing terms** (within DO/DOCa) — minimum time before release:

| Term | Red | White / Rosé |
|---|---|---|
| **Joven** ("young") | No/minimal oak, for early drinking | No/minimal oak |
| **Crianza** | 2 yrs total, ≥6 months in oak | 6 months in oak |
| **Reserva** | 3 yrs total, ≥1 yr in oak | 2 yrs total, ≥6 months in oak |
| **Gran Reserva** | 5 yrs total (≥2 yrs oak + 3 yrs bottle) | 4 yrs total, ≥6 months in oak |

### Germany — ripeness-based ladder

Classification is built on **grape-must ripeness at harvest**, measured on the **Oechsle scale (°Oe)** (sugar content).

| Tier | EU sense | Key facts |
|---|---|---|
| **Deutscher Wein** | — | Basic; 100% German origin |
| **Landwein** | ≈ PGI | From one of 26 Landwein regions; must be **Trocken** (dry) or **Halbtrocken** (off-dry) |
| **Qualitätswein (QbA)** | quality | From one of 13 Anbaugebiete; **chaptalization permitted**; min 51–72°Oe; min 7% alcohol |
| **Prädikatswein** | top | **No chaptalization**; from 39 Bereich; assigned a Prädikat by ripeness |

**Prädikat ladder (ascending ripeness):**

| Prädikat | Min. °Oe | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Kabinett** | 67–82 | Fully ripe main harvest; lightest, dry to off-dry |
| **Spätlese** | 76–90 | "Late harvest", picked ≥7 days after normal date; dry to medium-sweet |
| **Auslese** | 83–100 | "Selected harvest", overripe bunches; semi-sweet to sweet; some noble rot possible |
| **Beerenauslese (BA)** | 110–128 | "Berry selected", individually picked, usually noble rot; lusciously sweet |
| **Trockenbeerenauslese (TBA)** | 150–154 | "Dried berry selected", botrytis-shrivelled raisins; among the rarest/priciest wines |
| **Eiswein** | 110–128 (= BA) | Grapes pressed while frozen on the vine; always sweet; makeable in years with **no** noble rot |

**Sweetness terms:** **Trocken** (dry, RS ≤4 g/L, up to 9 with the acidity rule); **Halbtrocken** (off-dry, RS ≤12 g/L); **Lieblich** (medium-sweet) and **Süß** (sweet). When no sweetness term appears on a Prädikatswein label, the wine is typically sweet.

### Cross-cutting label terms

- **Late Harvest** (Fr. *Vendange Tardive*, Ger. *Spätlese/Auslese*) — grapes left on the vine past the normal date; richer, often sweeter.
- **Noble Rot** (*Botrytis cinerea*) — benevolent fungus that concentrates sugar/acid/flavour; honeyed, apricot, marmalade character. Examples: Sauternes, German BA/TBA, Tokaji Aszú.
- **Icewine / Eiswein** — frozen grapes pressed to leave concentrated juice; high RS balanced by high acidity. In Canada "Icewine" is a trademarked single word.
- **Vintage** — grapes (typically ≥85–95%) harvested in the stated year. **Non-vintage (NV)** wines (common in Champagne, some fortified) are blended across years for consistency.
- **Old Vine** (*Vieilles Vignes* / *Alte Reben*, "V.V.") — **not legally regulated** anywhere; usually implies vines ~25–100+ years old, giving smaller, more concentrated yields.

## Implications

- **Read a label as a style/quality signal.** A narrow place (Grand Cru, Classico, DOCa) and a higher tier mean tighter rules and usually higher quality. A printed grape variety, vintage, or "Reserva/Riserva" tells you the producer is allowed to make that claim under the appellation's rules.
- **Old World = place-led; New World = grape-led.** Old World labels lead with the region (and you must *know* the region's grapes); New World labels print the grape on the front. Knowing the place-to-grape map (Burgundy → Chardonnay/Pinot, Barolo → Nebbiolo, Rioja → Tempranillo) is exactly what the exam tests.
- **Sweetness can be inferred.** A German Prädikatswein with no sweetness term is typically sweet; Trocken/Halbtrocken flag dry/off-dry. The higher the Prädikat (BA, TBA), the sweeter.
- **Ageing terms map to time, not just quality.** Gran Reserva > Reserva > Crianza in bottle/oak time; Riserva and Superiore signal extra ageing/alcohol.

## Misconceptions

- **"PDO and PGI are the same strictness."** No — PDO requires *all* raw material and *every* production stage in the region; PGI needs only *one* stage and not all raw material from the area.
- **"AOC and AOP are different quality levels."** They are legally equivalent for wine; "AOC" is the traditional French mention, "AOP" the EU rendering.
- **"DOCG outranks DOC because it's older."** A DOCG must *first* have been a DOC (and a DOC must have been IGT for ≥5 years) — it is promoted upward, not separate.
- **"Burgundy Grand Cru labels show the village."** Grand Cru labels show **only** the vineyard name plus "Grand Cru"; the village name is omitted.
- **"Old Vine" guarantees a minimum age.** It is not legally regulated in any major region — there is no official minimum vine age.
