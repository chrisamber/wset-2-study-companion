# Sangiovese

## Analogy

Sangiovese is the wine equivalent of a sharp tomato-based pasta sauce — high-acid, savoury, herbal, with sour-cherry fruit rather than sweet jam. It's lean and grippy rather than plush, which is exactly why it sits so naturally next to Italian food. And like that sauce, the *same* grape tastes very different depending on where it's cooked: bright and zippy in the cool north (Chianti), rich and brooding in the warmer south (Brunello di Montalcino).

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

| Attribute | Profile |
|-----------|---------|
| **Colour** | Light to medium ruby — the grape lacks acylated anthocyanins, the phenolics needed for deep colour |
| **Body** | Medium to full (varies by region: light in Chianti, full in Brunello) |
| **Acidity** | High |
| **Tannin** | Medium-plus / medium to medium-high (firm) |
| **Aromas & flavours** | Sour red cherry, strawberry, tomato leaf/tomato-savouriness, savoury herbs (fennel, rosemary, thyme), violets, tea leaf, iron, balsamic, clay/dark-rock minerality; leather and tobacco develop with age |

Young examples show fresh strawberry and a little spice. Tuscan versions have a distinctive bitter-sweet cherry, violet and tea character with tomato-savouriness in youth.

### Clones — why this matters for the regions

- **Sangiovese Grosso** — higher quality; includes the **Brunello** clone (Brunello di Montalcino) and **Prugnolo Gentile** (Vino Nobile di Montepulciano).
- **Sangiovese Piccolo** — more common, lower quality; the base of most Chianti.

At least fourteen clones exist; in 1879 Italian authorities confirmed "Brunello" was identical to Sangiovese.

### How climate and winemaking shape style

- **Climate:** Cooler, northern sites (Chianti) give more acidity and less body; warmer, drier southern sites (Brunello di Montalcino — one of the warmest, driest parts of Tuscany) give the fullest-bodied, longest-lived wines.
- **Extraction:** Extended maceration (3–4 weeks vs a typical 7–12 days) is used to pull out phenols.
- **Oak — two philosophies:** Traditional large old casks (botti, ~5–6 hL; Slavonian oak in Brunello) impart minimal oak flavour, giving austere wines with pronounced fruit, earth and tannin. Modern new French oak/barriques give richer, toasty, vanilla-driven wines — Sangiovese acts as "a sponge" for oak compounds. New oak during malolactic fermentation softens and rounds the tannins.

### Key regions and styles

| Region (all DOCG) | Sangiovese min. | Style |
|-------------------|-----------------|-------|
| **Chianti** | 75–100% (≥80% in practice) | Largest, most northern; higher acidity, less body; mostly for young drinking |
| **Chianti Classico** | 80% | Historic heartland (between Florence and Siena); medium-bodied, firm tannin, more age-worthy — top Riserva/Gran Selezione rival Brunello |
| **Brunello di Montalcino** | 100% (Brunello clone) | Southern; fullest-bodied, most tannic, complex and longest-lived; fleshier texture, blackberry/black cherry/chocolate/leather/violets |
| **Vino Nobile di Montepulciano** | 70% (Prugnolo Gentile) | Long-aged; min. 2 yrs (3 for Riserva), 12–24 months in oak |
| **Carmignano** | 50%, blended with 10–20% Cabernet | Darker fruit, more structure than Chianti; first DOC approved for Cabernet blending (1975) |
| **Morellino di Scansano** | — | Southern Maremma (called Morellino); plusher, juicier, softer acidity |

Also grown widely in Umbria and Marche, and in Emilia-Romagna as Sangiovese di Romagna.

### Labelling / classification terms

- **DOCG** — Italy's top quality tier; all the regions above are DOCG.
- **Classico** — wine from the historic Chianti Classico zone (between Florence and Siena), under stricter rules than basic Chianti DOCG.
- **Riserva** — extended ageing. Chianti Classico Riserva = min. 24 months at the winery (vs 7 months for the standard Annata). Brunello Riserva = released ~62 months from harvest (vs ~50 for Normale).
- **Gran Selezione** — Chianti Classico's top tier (introduced 2014), made exclusively from a single estate's own grapes.
- **Blending rules:** Chianti permits up to 15% Cabernet Sauvignon (and other supporting reds such as Merlot, Syrah, plus Canaiolo); white grapes are banned in Chianti Classico since 2006.

### Ageing potential

- Basic Chianti: 3–4 years.
- Chianti Classico Riserva: 15+ years.
- Vino Nobile / Rosso di Montalcino: ~5 years, up to 8–10.
- Brunello di Montalcino / Super Tuscans: 20+ years in great vintages; most need ~10 years to harmonise.
- New World examples: typically peak within 3–4 years.

## Implications

- **Blind-tasting tells:** high acidity + firm (medium-plus) tannin + *light-to-medium ruby* colour + sour cherry, dried herbs and tomato-leaf savouriness ⇒ think Sangiovese. The savoury tomato/herb note and high acid are the classic giveaways.
- **Region → grape associations to drill:** Chianti → Sangiovese; Chianti Classico → Sangiovese; Brunello di Montalcino → 100% Sangiovese; Vino Nobile di Montepulciano → Sangiovese (Prugnolo Gentile); Morellino di Scansano → Sangiovese (Morellino).
- **Labelling logic:** "Classico" = the historic heartland, not just any Chianti; "Riserva" = longer ageing; "100% Sangiovese" points to Brunello.
- **Service/food:** the high acid and savoury herbal profile make it a natural partner for Italian, tomato-based dishes (acid in the wine mirrors acid in the sauce).

## Misconceptions

- **"Sangiovese is a deeply coloured red."** No — it gives light-to-medium ruby wines because it lacks acylated anthocyanins.
- **"Brunello is a different grape from Sangiovese."** No — Brunello is a Sangiovese clone (Sangiovese Grosso); confirmed identical to Sangiovese in 1879.
- **"Chianti and Chianti Classico are the same wine."** No — Classico is the historic heartland (≥80% Sangiovese, more age-worthy) under stricter rules than the broader Chianti DOCG.
- **"Riserva just means older/better grapes."** No — it means extended ageing (e.g. min. 24 months at the winery for Chianti Classico Riserva vs 7 for Annata).
- **"Chianti must be 100% Sangiovese."** No — Chianti requires roughly 75–80% minimum and permits up to 15% Cabernet Sauvignon and other supporting varieties; only Brunello di Montalcino is 100%.
