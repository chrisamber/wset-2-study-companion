# Grenache / Garnacha

## Analogy

Grenache is the "fruit roll-up" grape — its red fruit is so ripe and sweet-smelling it tastes almost candied. Think of it as the warm, sunny, easy-going member of a band: it brings the body, the high alcohol, and the cheerful strawberry-raspberry tune, but it travels light on colour and tannin. That's exactly why it loves to play in a group (the GSM blend), where Syrah and Mourvèdre supply the structure and edge it lacks on its own.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

Grenache (Garnacha in Spain) is one of the most widely planted red grapes in the world. It is **thin-skinned** and **ripens late**, so it needs **hot, dry conditions** to ripen fully. Because it accumulates very high sugar, it can reach **substantial alcohol levels, often at least 15% ABV**.

| Structural trait | Grenache profile |
|---|---|
| Colour | Light to medium ruby; prone to browning / "bricking" at the rim, even when young |
| Body | Full |
| Tannin | Low to medium, soft |
| Acidity | Low to medium |
| Alcohol | Medium-plus to high (typically 13.5–16% ABV) |
| Aromas / flavours | Red fruit (raspberry, strawberry), white pepper spice, cinnamon; candied "fruit roll-up" character; develops leather and tar with age |

A useful shorthand from the source: "Wines made from Grenache tend to lack acid, tannin and colour" — but make up for it with ripe fruit, body and alcohol. Grenache is also **highly prone to oxidation**, which is why even young wines can show an orange/brick tinge, especially from hot climates.

### How climate and winemaking shape the style

Because Grenache needs heat to ripen, its classic homes are warm: as a thin-skinned, low-tannin, low-acid grape it relies on full ripeness for its high sugar (hence high alcohol) and ripe red fruit. Its thin skin also makes it useful for **pale rosé and light-coloured reds**, and its high sugar makes it suitable for **sweet fortified wines**.

The single most important winemaking decision is **blending**. Grenache is the backbone of the **GSM blend** (Grenache / Syrah / Mourvèdre), the classic Southern Rhône red style:

- **Grenache** contributes ripe red/candied fruit, body and high alcohol (a "long tingly finish"), without heavy tannin.
- **Syrah** ([[syrah-shiraz]]) adds pepper, dark fruit, structure and deeper colour.
- **Mourvèdre** adds earthiness, tannin backbone and savoury character.

It is also blended with Carignan, Tempranillo and Cinsaut. The result: GSM wines are "rich, full bodied and leathery," with dark fruit and spice.

### Key regions and styles

| Region (country) | Style notes |
|---|---|
| Châteauneuf-du-Pape (FR, S. Rhône) | Grenache typically >80% of the blend; spicy richness; full-bodied; high alcohol. Appellation allows up to 18 grape varieties |
| Côtes du Rhône / Côtes du Rhône Villages (FR) | GSM-dominant; "chewy fruitiness," approachable. Villages sits above basic Côtes du Rhône, with lower yields and higher minimum alcohol |
| Languedoc-Roussillon inc. Minervois (FR) | Grenache blended with Syrah and Carignan; warm, ripe style. Basis of fortified vins doux naturels in Roussillon (Rivesaltes, Banyuls, Maury) |
| Rioja (ES) | Grown in the warmer Rioja Baja; blended with Tempranillo, adding "juicy fruitiness and body" |
| Navarra (ES) | Garnacha is 54% of plantings (dominant); varietal Garnacha plus dry rosado; fruity, early-drinking |
| Priorat (ES) | Old-vine Garnacha on *llicorella* slate; dense, concentrated, cellar-worthy wines with noticeable tannin and dark colour — unlike the lighter norm. Holds DOQ status |
| Barossa Valley (AU) | Old-vine GSM; high alcohol, ripe dark/red fruit |
| McLaren Vale (AU) | Old-vine GSM; plush texture, ripe fruit, chocolate notes |

Spain is the variety's likely homeland (Aragon, in the northeast, has Spain's highest concentration). It is Spain's third most-planted red grape (after Tempranillo and Bobal). In Australia, where some plantings are over 100 years old, Grenache is now grown mainly in South Australia (also Clare Valley) and historically formed the basis of most Australian fortified wine.

### Labelling terms

- **France:** called **Grenache** (or Grenache Noir). Wines are labelled by **appellation** (e.g. Châteauneuf-du-Pape, Côtes du Rhône), not by grape.
- **Spain:** called **Garnacha** (or Garnacha Tinta). Labelled by variety **or** appellation (Rioja, Navarra, Priorat). In Catalonia / Priorat the Catalan spelling is **Garnatxa**.
- **Australia:** labelled by variety (**Grenache**) or as **GSM** blends.

## Implications

- **Synonym trap:** Grenache (France/Australia) and Garnacha (Spain) are the **same grape** — a classic exam association. Garnatxa (Catalan) is the same grape again.
- **Blind-tasting tells:** light/medium ruby colour with a possible brick/orange rim even when young + soft low-medium tannin + low-medium acidity + ripe candied red fruit and white pepper + noticeably **high alcohol** points to Grenache or a GSM blend.
- **GSM logic for the exam:** remember what each grape brings — Grenache = fruit/body/alcohol, Syrah = pepper/colour/structure, Mourvèdre = tannin/earth. Châteauneuf-du-Pape is the benchmark (Grenache >80%).
- **Versatility:** the same grape makes dry reds, dry rosés (e.g. Navarra rosado, Tavel) and sweet fortified wines — handy when a question lists multiple style possibilities for one variety.
- **Regional exception:** Priorat is the "odd one out" — old-vine Garnacha on slate gives a dense, tannic, dark style unlike Grenache's usual lightness.

## Misconceptions

- **"Grenache is a deeply coloured, tannic red."** No — it is thin-skinned, so typically light-to-medium ruby with low-to-medium soft tannin (Priorat old vines are the exception).
- **"Grenache and Garnacha are different grapes."** They are the same variety; Garnacha is simply the Spanish name (Garnatxa in Catalan).
- **"It's a cool-climate grape."** It ripens late and needs hot, dry conditions, which is why its homes are warm regions like the Southern Rhône, Spain and South Australia.
- **"Grenache wines are low in alcohol because they taste fruity and soft."** The opposite — its high sugar gives substantial alcohol, often 15%+ ABV.
- **"A brick/orange tinge means the wine is faulty or over-aged."** Grenache oxidises and browns easily, so even young examples can show this rim colour.
