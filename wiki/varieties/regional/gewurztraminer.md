# Gewurztraminer

## Analogy

Gewurztraminer is the perfume-counter sample strip of the wine world: one sniff and you *know* it. Lychee and rose petal hit you before you've even tasted it — it literally shares its key aroma compounds with the lychee fruit. The name itself is a giveaway: *Gewürz* is German for "spice," tacked onto *Traminer* (a village in South Tyrol). So the grape tells you what it smells like right on the label — spicy, floral, and unmistakably loud.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

Despite making a *white* wine, Gewurztraminer has a **pink-to-red skin** — that deep pigment is why the wine often pours a notably deep golden, almost amber-tinged colour for a white. It carries **high natural sugar**, which feeds both its high alcohol and its sweet styles.

| Attribute | Gewurztraminer |
|---|---|
| Skin colour | Pink to red (still a white-wine variety) |
| Wine colour in glass | Medium to deep golden / amber-tinged for a white |
| Acidity | Low |
| Body | Medium to full |
| Alcohol | High (and can read even higher because of the aromatic intensity) |
| Aroma intensity | Pronounced / intensely aromatic |
| Primary aromas | Lychee, rose petal, baking spice, ginger, passion fruit |

The combination of **low acidity + heightened aromatics + residual sugar** means even bone-dry bottlings can taste sweeter than they actually are.

### How climate and soil shape it

Gewurztraminer performs best in **cooler climates**. Hot climates cause a sugar–acidity imbalance — the grape piles on sugar but loses the acid needed to stay balanced — so warmth is the enemy here. Soil matters too:

- **Fine siliceous soils** → increase the spicy character and the wine's ageing ability.
- **Coarse dolomitic soils** → produce more floral, lighter expressions.

### Style range (driest to sweetest)

The grape's high natural sugar lets it span dry table wine through to lavish dessert wine:

1. **Dry** — low residual sugar but still rich and full-bodied with pronounced aromatics (e.g. Maison Trimbach makes very dry expressions).
2. **Off-dry** — the most common standard/commercial style; lychee and rose at the fore.
3. **Vendanges Tardives (VT)** — late-harvest; rich and sweet; minimum **257 g/L** sugar at harvest.
4. **Sélection de Grains Nobles (SGN)** — noble-rot (botrytis) affected; the richest, most concentrated style; minimum **306 g/L** sugar at harvest.

### Key regions / GIs

| Region / GI | Style & significance |
|---|---|
| **Alsace, France** | The definitive home — over **25% of the world's plantings**; the grape most characteristic of the region; styles from dry through off-dry to SGN dessert wines. |
| **Alsace Grand Cru** | Wines from single delimited plots with superior terroir (50 designated sites); Gewurztraminer is one of **four noble varieties** authorised (with Riesling, Muscat and Pinot Gris); greater concentration and complexity. |

### Labelling

Alsace wines — including Grand Cru — are labelled by **grape variety** on the front label. This is unusual for France, where most appellations use regional or village names instead. Grand Cru wines additionally carry the name of the individual vineyard site.

## Implications

- **One of the easiest wines to call blind:** the signature combo is **lychee + rose + spice, low acid, high alcohol, deep golden colour**. If a tasting note screams "lychee," think Gewurztraminer first.
- **Drill the association:** Alsace → Gewurztraminer (one of its four noble grapes). It is the variety *most characteristic* of Alsace.
- **Service / food:** its aromatic richness and off-dry styles make it a classic match for **spicy and Asian cuisines** (Thai, Indian, Moroccan tagine, ceviche), bittersweet dishes, and fresh white cheeses; sweeter VT/SGN styles pair with desserts featuring cinnamon, honey, dried nuts and dried fruit.
- **Remember the perception trap:** because of low acid plus aromatic lift, dry Gewurztraminer can taste sweeter than its sugar level suggests — don't assume "tastes sweet" means "is sweet."

## Misconceptions

- **"It's a red wine grape because the skins are pink/red."** No — it's classed as a white-wine variety; the pigment just gives the wine an unusually deep golden colour.
- **"If it tastes sweet, it must be a sweet wine."** Low acidity + intense aromatics make even dry versions read as sweet; sweetness perception ≠ residual sugar.
- **"Gewurztraminer loves warm, sunny climates like other rich whites."** It actually performs best in *cooler* climates; heat throws its sugar and acidity out of balance.
- **"You can't tell where an Alsace wine's grape is — French labels hide it."** Alsace is the exception: it labels by grape variety on the front, so the bottle says "Gewurztraminer."
- **"VT and SGN are just marketing tiers."** They are defined by minimum harvest-sugar thresholds (VT 257 g/L, SGN 306 g/L, the latter botrytis-affected) — they denote progressively sweeter, more concentrated styles.
