# Tempranillo

## Analogy

Tempranillo is the session musician of Spanish reds — on its own it's structurally neutral (low acid and sugar in its base form), so it's built to take direction. Hand it an oak barrel and a few blending partners and it becomes the lead: vanilla and coconut from American oak, backbone from Garnacha or Graciano. Its name even means "the early one" (from *temprano*, Spanish for "early"), because it ripens weeks ahead of most Spanish reds.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

Spain's noble red grape. Thin skins, large bunches; wines range from medium ruby to garnet. In its base form it is **low in both acidity and sugar**, which is *why* it leans so heavily on oak and blending — in practice, finished Rioja shows medium tannin and medium acidity, while VinePair describes the wine more broadly as full-bodied with plenty of tannin and acidity.

| Feature | Typical profile |
|---|---|
| Colour | Medium to deep ruby / garnet |
| Acidity | Medium (low without oak/blending) |
| Tannin | Medium to medium-high |
| Alcohol | Medium to high |
| Primary flavours | Strawberry, cherry, plum, tobacco, leather, herbs, red flowers, earth |
| Oak-influenced flavours | Vanilla, coconut, dill, baking spice (American oak); cedar, finer spice (French oak) |

### How climate shapes the style

- Higher-elevation, cooler zones (e.g. Rioja Alta) → shorter growing seasons → **brighter fruit** and firmer structure.
- Lower-altitude, warmer zones → **deeper colour** and **higher alcohol**.
- High altitude with big diurnal swings (warm days, cool nights) **preserves acidity** — the mechanism behind Ribera del Duero's structure.

### How winemaking shapes the style — oak and blending

- **Oak ageing is the defining move.** Tempranillo "easily takes on the flavor of the barrel." **American oak** (historically dominant in Rioja) gives pronounced vanilla, coconut and dill; **French oak** (introduced by Bordeaux négociants, increasingly used today) gives finer tannin integration and spice. Modern producers often blend both.
- **Blending:** Rioja reds require a minimum 95% from authorised traditional varieties — Tempranillo, **Garnacha** (Grenache), Graciano and Mazuelo (Carignan) — though monovarietal Tempranillo is increasingly common. Ribera del Duero is 90–100% Tinto Fino, sometimes complemented with Cabernet Sauvignon, Malbec and Merlot.

### Regional synonyms (same grape, different names)

| Synonym | Region |
|---|---|
| Tinto Fino / Tinta del País | Ribera del Duero |
| Cencibel | La Mancha, Valdepeñas |
| Tinta de Toro | Toro |
| Ull de Llebre | Catalunya |

### Key regions / GIs and their styles

| Region (GI) | Style |
|---|---|
| **Rioja** (DOCa, Spain's highest tier) | Tempranillo = 87.67% of red plantings; typically lighter, red-fruited (strawberry, cherry), more aromatic; medium to full body |
| **Ribera del Duero** (DOP, Castile y León; 750–911 m) | Local name Tinto Fino; bold, structured, ageable; deeper colour, dark fruit (blackberry, black cherry), higher tannin |
| **Toro** | Hotter subregion; grape ripens easily; dark-fruited, full-bodied, more extracted and powerful than Rioja |
| **Penedès / Catalunya** | Known as Ull de Llebre; lighter, more aromatic (Mediterranean influence) |
| Navarra, Valdepeñas | Secondary Tempranillo regions |

Rioja's own sub-zones: **Rioja Alta** (higher, brighter fruit, firmer structure), **Rioja Alavesa** (fuller body, higher acidity), **Rioja Oriental** (warmest, deepest colour, higher alcohol).

### Labelling / ageing terms

The Spanish ageing ladder is a high-value exam target — and the rules differ between Rioja and Ribera del Duero. See [[wine-labelling-and-geographical-indications]].

| Label | Rioja requirements | Ribera del Duero requirements |
|---|---|---|
| **Joven** ("young") | No oak required; released the year after harvest | (not commonly used) |
| **Crianza** | 2 years total; min. 1 yr in oak | 2 years total; min. 12 months oak |
| **Reserva** | 36 months total; min. 12 months oak | 3 years total; min. 12 months oak |
| **Gran Reserva** | Min. 24 months oak + 36 months bottle (60 months / 5 yrs total) | 5 years total; min. 2 years oak |

## Implications

- **Region → grape association:** Rioja → Tempranillo; Ribera del Duero → Tempranillo (under the name **Tinto Fino**). If a label says Tinto Fino, Tinta del País, Cencibel, Tinta de Toro or Ull de Llebre, it's still Tempranillo.
- **Blind-tasting tells:** strawberry/cherry red fruit plus tobacco and leather, with pronounced **vanilla, coconut and dill** from American oak, points to oak-aged Rioja. Deeper colour, blackberry/black-cherry dark fruit and firmer tannin points to Ribera del Duero.
- **Label = ageing, not always quality of grape:** a Gran Reserva tells the customer this wine has had years of oak and bottle age before release — useful when guiding a guest toward a softer, more developed style.
- **Garnacha is the classic Rioja blending partner** — see [[grenache-garnacha]].

## Misconceptions

- **"Tempranillo is naturally high in acid and tannin."** In its base form it's actually low in both acidity and sugar — its structure largely comes from oak and blending.
- **"Tinto Fino is a different grape from Tempranillo."** It's the same grape; Tinto Fino (and Tinta del País) is simply its Ribera del Duero name.
- **"Rioja and Ribera del Duero taste the same because it's the same grape."** Ribera's higher altitude and extreme continental climate give deeper colour, darker fruit and higher tannin than Rioja's lighter, red-fruited, more aromatic style.
- **"The coconut and vanilla mean it's a sweet wine."** Those are American-oak flavours, not sugar.
- **"Reserva always means more oak than Crianza by the same rule everywhere."** The minimums differ by region — e.g. Rioja Reserva needs 12 months oak within 36 months total, while Ribera del Duero Reserva needs 12 months oak within 3 years.
