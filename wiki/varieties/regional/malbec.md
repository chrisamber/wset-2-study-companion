# Malbec

## Analogy

Malbec is the immigrant who became a national hero. In its French homeland it was always a supporting player — frost-prone, disease-prone, never quite the star. Shipped to the high deserts of Argentina in 1868, it finally found the sun, heat, and altitude it needed and became Argentina's defining grape. So Malbec has two accents: the dark, rustic, structured French Cahors voice, and the plush, fruit-forward, velvety Argentine one. Same grape, two personalities — and the exam loves to test whether you can tell them apart.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

- **Colour:** Inky dark purple/violet — high phenolic content gives deep, almost black colour.
- **Body:** Full-bodied.
- **Tannin:** Robust and substantial in French Cahors; softer, riper and more velvety in Argentina.
- **Acidity:** Moderate (medium). In very warm regions lacking natural acidity, wines risk being "flabby and weak."
- **Alcohol:** Medium–high.
- **Typical aromas/flavours:** Plum, blackberry, black cherry, dark/black currant, with violet florals; cocoa and (with oak) vanilla and coconut. Cahors expressions lean to damson, tobacco, earth, garlic, raisin and cedar.

It is a **thick-skinned** grape that needs **more sun and heat than Cabernet Sauvignon or Merlot to ripen**, ripens mid-season, and is very susceptible to frost, coulure (shattering), downy mildew and rot — which is why it declined in Bordeaux.

### How climate and altitude shape the style

In Mendoza, Argentina, the premier zones — **Luján de Cuyo and Uco Valley** — sit in the Andean foothills at roughly **800–1,500 m elevation**. High altitude creates a large **diurnal (day/night) temperature swing** that preserves acidity and freshness while the fruit ripens fully and concentrates. The hot, high, dry climate also neutralises Malbec's disease weaknesses that plagued it in France. Cahors, by contrast, builds its dark, tannic expression on **limestone-based soils**.

### Key winemaking decisions

- **Oak:** In Cahors, oak ageing is traditional and adds tobacco, cedar and earthy complexity. In Argentina, both unoaked fruit-forward styles and oak-aged styles (American or French oak, adding vanilla/coconut) are made.
- **Blending vs varietal:** Cahors growers traditionally blended Malbec with Merlot and Tannat for dark, full-bodied wines, but increasingly make 100% varietal Malbec. Argentina makes both varietal Malbec and Bordeaux-style blends.
- **Training/yield:** Bush vine (gobelet) training at low yields (~6 tonnes/hectare) produces rich, dark, juicy wines.

### Regions and styles

| Region / GI | Style |
|---|---|
| **Mendoza, Argentina** (Luján de Cuyo, Uco Valley, 800–1,500 m) | World's leading producer; deep colour, intense juicy fruit (plum, blackberry, blueberry, dark cherry), violet, cocoa, velvety texture; medium–high but ripe/soft tannin; plush, approachable; high-altitude examples are the most highly rated, with medium–high ageing potential |
| **Cahors, France** | Malbec's traditional French stronghold; very deep, almost black; high, firm tannin; dark berry, tobacco, earth, cedar; structured, rustic, meaty; high ageing potential |
| **Chile** (Maule, Colchagua, Biobío) | Fresher, more fruity due to earlier harvest |
| **USA** (Napa, Paso Robles, Sonoma; Washington's Columbia Valley, Lake Chelan) | Washington examples: dark fruit and herbal aromas, moderate acidity and tannin |
| **France beyond Cahors** | Permitted blending grape in Bordeaux AOC; grown in the Loire (as Côt) and other southwest appellations |

### Labelling terms

- In Argentina and internationally the grape is labelled simply as **Malbec**.
- In **Cahors** the wine is labelled by the **appellation name (Cahors AOC)**, not by grape; AOC law requires a **minimum 70% Malbec** in the blend, and Malbec is Cahors' dominant red variety.
- **Synonyms:** Auxerrois (local name in Cahors — unrelated to the white Auxerrois blanc), Côt (Loire), Pressac (Bordeaux/Saint-Émilion), plus Côt Noir, Cauli, Piperdy. Galet documented over 1,000 synonyms in total.

## Implications

- **Region → grape associations to drill:** **Mendoza → Malbec** and **Cahors → Malbec**. These are direct exam questions.
- **Blind-tasting tells:** Inky-dark, full-bodied red with plum/blackberry/violet, cocoa and a smooth velvety texture → Argentine Malbec. A darker, firmer, more tannic, earthy/rustic, tobacco-and-cedar red → Cahors Malbec.
- **Altitude as the Argentine signature:** if a deeply fruity yet fresh, high-acid red is described as coming from high-altitude Andean foothills, think Malbec.
- **Service/food:** pairs naturally with red meat, especially smoky or spice-rubbed preparations — Argentine asado is the classic match; also roasted mushrooms, spiced bean dishes, and umami-forward cheeses (manchego, sharp cheddar, stilton).
- Remember Malbec needs **more sun/heat than Cabernet Sauvignon or Merlot** — a useful ripening contrast.

## Misconceptions

- **"Malbec is an Argentine grape."** It originated in France (Côt); it was only introduced to Argentina by Michel Pouget in 1868 and became its national variety.
- **"Cahors and Mendoza Malbec taste the same."** Cahors is darker, firmer-tannined, rustic and earthy; Argentine Malbec is plusher, softer and more fruit-forward.
- **"A Cahors label tells you the grape."** Cahors is labelled by appellation, not variety — but by law it must be at least 70% Malbec.
- **"Malbec is easy to grow."** It is very disease-prone (frost, coulure, downy mildew, rot), which is exactly why it declined in Bordeaux after the 1956 frost, replaced by Merlot and Cabernet Franc.
- **"Auxerrois in Cahors is a white grape."** Auxerrois is the local synonym for Malbec in Cahors — not the unrelated white Auxerrois blanc.
