# Chenin Blanc

## Analogy

Chenin Blanc is the session musician who can play any gig. Hand it a cool Loire vineyard and it lays down a bone-dry, mineral track; leave the grapes on the vine through an autumn mist and it turns into a lush, century-ageing dessert wine; under-ripen it on purpose and it becomes the fizz in your glass. One grape, the full set list — dry, off-dry, sweet, and sparkling — and the thing holding every style together is its high natural acidity, the steady backbeat that keeps even the sweetest version from collapsing into cloying mush.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

- **Colour:** white grape (yellow-green berries).
- **Acidity:** **high** natural acidity — its defining feature. This acidity acts as a natural preservative, letting it span dry → off-dry → sweet → sparkling while staying balanced. Picked too early, that same acidity makes "one of the nastiest wines possible."
- **Body:** ranges from light to medium (e.g. Vouvray) up to full-bodied (Savennières); depends on style and region.
- **Alcohol:** style-dependent — dry Savennières ~13.5%; sweet dessert styles typically kept **below 12%** to preserve the sweetness-acid balance.
- **Aromas/flavours:** apple, greengage, quince, honey, acacia, white flowers, angelica, chalky/flinty minerals (cool climate); with age develops honey, quince, marzipan, barley sugar. Warm-climate versions show tropical fruit — pineapple, banana, guava, pear, melon, passionfruit.

### How climate and winemaking shape the styles

Chenin is naturally vigorous, breaks bud early and ripens late with thin skins — which makes it prone to **noble rot (Botrytis cinerea)**, deliberately exploited in the Loire for dessert wines.

| Style | Loire term | How it's made / conditions |
|-------|-----------|----------------------------|
| Dry | **Sec** | Cooler years; windier, drier sites where botrytis struggles (Savennières) |
| Off-dry | **Demi-Sec** | Honeyed, floral; develops with age (Vouvray, Montlouis) |
| Sweet | **Moelleux / Doux** | Best vintages; grapes left to develop noble rot → intense, viscous dessert wine |
| Sparkling | **Pétillant (semi) / Mousseux (fully)** | High acidity suits traditional method; under-ripe grapes used (Crémant de Loire, sparkling Vouvray) |

Winemaking choices:
- **Cooler vintages** push toward dry and sparkling; **warmer vintages** toward sweet and botrytized.
- **Fermentation temp:** Loire (Old World) 16–20°C; South Africa (New World) often 10–12°C — lower temps preserve tropical fruit.
- **Malolactic fermentation (MLF):** softens acidity and adds creamy texture; Loire producers often avoid or partly block it to keep freshness.
- **Oak:** Old World tends to shy from new oak (Savennières historically used acacia and chestnut); New World may embrace oak's vanilla/spice.
- **Skin contact:** accommodates some maceration, emphasising greengage and angelica notes.
- **Yields matter:** low Loire yields (40–50 hl/ha) bring out floral, honeyed character; very high Californian yields (~175 hl/ha) give "bland and neutral" wine.

### Key regions and styles

| Region / GI | Country | Climate | Signature style |
|-------------|---------|---------|-----------------|
| **Vouvray AOC** | France (Loire) | Continental + Atlantic | Commonly off-dry; full range sec→doux, still + sparkling |
| **Savennières AOC** | France (Loire) | Warmer, drier, windier | Predominantly **dry**, full-bodied |
| **Coteaux du Layon AOC** | France (Loire) | More Atlantic mist | Sweet botrytized dessert (incl. Quarts de Chaume, Bonnezeaux) |
| **Anjou / Saumur** | France (Loire) | — | Green-fruit, steely; Saumur a base for Crémant de Loire sparkling |
| **Montlouis-sur-Loire** | France (Loire) | Like Vouvray, south bank | Sec→moelleux, still + sparkling |
| **Stellenbosch / Swartland / Paarl** | South Africa | Warm | Rich, concentrated, tropical fruit; often young; old-vine premium |

South Africa is the grape's stronghold — **the most widely planted variety there (~18.6% of plantings)**, with more Chenin than the rest of the world combined, and nearly twice French acreage. Known locally as **Steen**, it arrived in the 17th century.

### Labelling terms

- France: Loire Chenin is labelled by **AOC** (Vouvray, Savennières, Coteaux du Layon) — the grape is implied, not named.
- South Africa: usually labelled "**Chenin Blanc**"; older labels may say "**Steen**."
- Vouvray sweetness scale (by residual sugar): **Sec** (0–0.4%) → **Demi-Sec** (0.4–1.2%) → **Moelleux** (1.2–4.5%) → **Doux** (4.5%+). The sweetness level is often **not stated** on the label, so vintage knowledge matters: cooler vintages = drier, warmer vintages = sweeter.

## Implications

- **Region → grape association (exam gold):** **Vouvray → Chenin Blanc**. Also Savennières, Coteaux du Layon, Anjou/Saumur, Montlouis → Chenin. South Africa's flagship white → Chenin Blanc (Steen).
- **Blind-tasting tells:** white with searingly high acidity plus apple/quince/honey and chalky minerals → think Loire Chenin; if it's rich and tropical (pineapple, guava, banana) → South African Chenin.
- **Versatility:** one grape that can be dry, off-dry, sweet, or sparkling — a likely "which grape can do all these styles?" question.
- **Ageability:** sweet Loire Chenin is among the longest-lived wines in the world (100+ years). Dry Vouvray-style can age 15–20+ years; sparkling is best within ~3 years. Watch for "dumb phases" where young wine closes up temporarily.
- **Service link:** as a high-acid white spanning dry to sweet, serving temperature and food-pairing logic mirror other crisp/aromatic whites (see [[wine-serving-temperature]]).

## Misconceptions

- **"Chenin Blanc is always sweet."** No — it spans dry (Savennières) through off-dry (Vouvray) to sweet (Coteaux du Layon) and sparkling; sweetness depends on style and vintage.
- **"Vouvray is one fixed style."** Vouvray runs the full range sec → demi-sec → moelleux → doux, plus pétillant and mousseux sparkling.
- **"You can read the sweetness off the label."** Often you can't — Vouvray frequently omits it, so vintage (cool = drier, warm = sweeter) is the clue.
- **"Steen is a different grape from Chenin Blanc."** Steen is South Africa's local name for Chenin Blanc, only confirmed as the same grape in 1965.
- **"South African Chenin tastes like Loire Chenin."** No — warm-climate South African versions are richer and tropical (pineapple, guava), versus the Loire's mineral, apple/quince character.
