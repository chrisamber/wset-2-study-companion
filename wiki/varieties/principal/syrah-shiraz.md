# Syrah / Shiraz

## Analogy

Syrah and Shiraz are the same grape wearing two different jackets. In the cool granite hills of the Northern Rhône it dresses formally — tannic, smoky, peppery, restrained, built to age. In the hot Barossa sun it swaps into shorts and a t-shirt — jammy, high-alcohol, soft-tannined, ready to drink young. Same person, opposite outfits, and the name on the label (Syrah vs Shiraz) usually tells you which jacket you're getting.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

A dark-skinned black grape producing red wines that are **high in tannin and high in acidity**. The general flavour profile runs from violets and berries through chocolate to black pepper, with coffee and pepper commonly noted. With bottle age it develops **leather, wet leaves and earth**.

The expression splits sharply by climate:

- **Moderate climate (e.g. Northern Rhône):** medium to full-bodied, medium-plus to high tannin, notes of blackberry, mint and black pepper; mineral, tannic, elegant, smoke-flavoured with restrained fruit.
- **Hot climate (e.g. Barossa Valley):** more consistently full-bodied, softer tannin, jammier fruit and spice notes of licorice, anise and earthy leather; higher alcohol, less obviously tannic, more easily approached when young.

### Syrah vs Shiraz — the name tells the style

As a general rule, most **Australian and South African** wines are labelled **"Shiraz"**, and most **European** wines (where varietal labelling is practised) are labelled **"Syrah"**. The Northern Rhône appellations have no tradition of varietal labelling — those wines are sold under the appellation name (Hermitage, Côte-Rôtie, Crozes-Hermitage), not the grape.

The convention carries a style implication: "Syrah"-labelled wines suggest the classic Northern Rhône profile (more elegant, tannic, smoke-flavoured, restrained fruit), while "Shiraz"-labelled wines suggest the New World archetype (riper, more fruit-driven, higher alcohol, less obviously tannic, peppery rather than smoky, easier to drink young).

### Winemaking — Viognier co-fermentation and oak

In the Northern Rhône's **Côte-Rôtie**, the white grape **Viognier** has long been co-fermented with Syrah, up to **20%** permitted, for aromatic lift. Some Australian Shiraz producers add up to about **4%** Viognier for apricot tones on the nose and palate. Côte-Rôtie was one of the first Rhône regions to use new oak barrels for ageing. In Australia, new **American oak** is common — as in **Penfolds Grange** (created by Max Schubert in 1951), predominantly Shiraz, sometimes with a small quantity of Cabernet Sauvignon.

### Region → style

| Region / GI | Climate | Style |
|---|---|---|
| **Hermitage** (N. Rhône) | Cool–moderate, granite hill | ~100% Syrah (up to 15% Marsanne/Roussanne allowed); earthy, leather, red berries, cocoa/coffee; high tannin; ages up to 40 years; "spiritual home of Syrah" |
| **Côte-Rôtie** (N. Rhône) | Cool–moderate | Syrah + up to 20% Viognier; green olive, raspberry, violets, meaty bacon, pepper; Côte Blonde (elegant, earlier) vs Côte Brune (more tannic, full-bodied, ages longer) |
| **Crozes-Hermitage** (N. Rhône) | Cool–moderate | Largest N. Rhône AOC; ~100% Syrah; lighter, earlier-drinking, more approachable and affordable; entry-level Northern Rhône Syrah |
| **Barossa Valley** (S. Australia) | Hot, semi-arid | Full body, softer tannin, high alcohol, deep purple-red; jammy blackberry/plum, licorice, anise, leather; old vines (from 1847); often American oak; Penfolds Grange benchmark |
| **Hunter Valley** (NSW) | Warm, humid | Medium-bodied Shiraz, earthy/leathery character; historically important Australian red region |
| **Margaret River** (W. Australia) | Cooler | Shiraz with marginally less alcohol, often in a more traditional French style |
| **South Eastern Australia** GI | Broad multi-state | Large-volume blends across NSW, Victoria, S. Australia, Tasmania |

### Labelling / classification

- Northern Rhône wines are **AOC** (Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée): bottles bear the appellation name and **do not state the grape variety** (standard French practice).
- New World examples almost universally say **"Shiraz"** on the label (varietal labelling).
- Australia introduced Syrah in **1832** (James Busby); it is now Australia's most popular red grape, and Australia is the world's second-largest Syrah/Shiraz grower after France.

## Implications

- **Blind-tasting tells:** high tannin + high acidity + **black pepper** is the classic Syrah signature. Smoky, restrained, mineral and tannic points to Northern Rhône; jammy, high-alcohol, soft-tannined with licorice/anise points to a hot-climate Shiraz (Barossa).
- **Region → grape drills:** Hermitage, Côte-Rôtie and Crozes-Hermitage all → Syrah. Barossa Valley → Shiraz. The label word ("Syrah" vs "Shiraz") is itself a clue to expected style.
- **Service:** Northern Rhône reds (especially Hermitage, Côte Brune) are built to age — Hermitage up to 40 years; Crozes-Hermitage and Barossa are more approachable young.
- **Côte-Rôtie's Viognier** is an easy exam catch: a white grape co-fermented into a red wine, up to 20%.

## Misconceptions

- **"Syrah and Shiraz are different grapes."** No — they are the same grape; the name signals region and expected style (Europe/"Syrah" vs Australia & South Africa/"Shiraz").
- **"Northern Rhône reds are labelled Syrah."** No — they are sold under the appellation name (Hermitage, Côte-Rôtie, Crozes-Hermitage); French AOCs here don't state the grape.
- **"All Syrah/Shiraz is a big, jammy, high-alcohol red."** No — that's the hot-climate Barossa style; cool–moderate Northern Rhône Syrah is more mineral, tannic and restrained.
- **"Côte-Rôtie is pure Syrah."** No — up to 20% Viognier (a white grape) may be co-fermented with the Syrah.
- **"Crozes-Hermitage and Hermitage are interchangeable."** No — Crozes-Hermitage is the larger, lighter, earlier-drinking entry-level appellation; Hermitage is the prestigious, age-worthy one.
