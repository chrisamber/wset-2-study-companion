# Merlot

## Analogy

Merlot is the diplomat of the Bordeaux grape line-up. Where Cabernet Sauvignon shows up in a stiff suit — firm, drying, slow to ripen — Merlot arrives soft, fleshy and approachable, ready two weeks early. That easy temperament is exactly why winemakers seat it next to Cabernet: it rounds off the sharp edges and fills out the middle. On its own (the Right Bank), it can still carry the table; in support (the Left Bank), it's the one smoothing the conversation.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

Merlot is a dark-blue-skinned black grape with **less blue/black hue and a thinner skin than Cabernet Sauvignon**, giving it fewer tannins per unit volume. It carries **higher sugar and lower malic acid** than Cabernet, and its signature is a velvety, plush texture — softer and less drying than Cabernet Sauvignon.

| Attribute | Typical value |
|---|---|
| Colour | Deep ruby / deep garnet |
| Tannin | Low–medium (softer than Cabernet Sauvignon) |
| Acidity | Medium |
| Body | Medium to full |
| Alcohol | Medium–high (rises with ripeness/climate) |
| Aromas/flavours (cool) | Strawberry, red cherry, raspberry, plum, cedar, tobacco, leafy/vegetal |
| Aromas/flavours (moderate) | Blackberry, black plum, black cherry, violet, chocolate |
| Aromas/flavours (hot/ripe) | Fruitcake, chocolate, jam |
| Oak influence | Vanilla, caramel, chocolate, coconut, coffee bean, walnut, baking spice |
| Ripening | ~2 weeks earlier than Cabernet Sauvignon; prone to rapid over-ripening |
| Preferred soils | Clay (cool, moisture-retaining); limestone adds freshness |

### How climate shapes the style

Merlot is a flavour thermometer — the fruit shifts from red to black to baked as the climate warms:

- **Cool climate / early harvest:** maintains acidity, with fresh red fruit (raspberry, strawberry) and potentially leafy, vegetal notes; develops grippier tannins and herbaceous, peppery notes, ending up structurally closer to Cabernet Sauvignon.
- **Moderate climate:** blackberry, black plum, black cherry.
- **Warm/hot climate / late harvest:** full body, high alcohol, juicier and jammier fruit; flavours run to fruitcake and chocolate.

### Key winemaking decisions

- **Harvest timing** is the central lever, captured by two philosophies: Château Pétrus favours **early picking** to keep acidity and finesse; consultant Michel Rolland favours **late picking** for the added fruit and body of slight over-ripeness.
- **Oak:** significant time in oak adds caramel, chocolate, coconut, coffee bean, vanilla and walnut; new oak in the New World brings vanilla and sweet baking spice.
- **Blending:** Merlot's softness, flesh and early ripening make it a natural partner for the sterner, later-ripening Cabernet Sauvignon. Classic Bordeaux blending partners are Cabernet Sauvignon, Cabernet Franc, Malbec and Petit Verdot.

### Key regions and their styles

| Region (GI) | Style |
|---|---|
| Saint-Émilion (Bordeaux Right Bank) | ~60% Merlot, blended with Cabernet Franc; limestone/clay soils give freshness, minerality and structure; blackcurrant, dark cherry, chocolate, spice |
| Pomerol (Bordeaux Right Bank) | ~80% Merlot with Cabernet Franc; iron-clay soils give a firmer tannic backbone; lush, plush, silky, with floral and meaty/chocolate notes with age |
| Médoc / Graves (Bordeaux Left Bank) | ~25% of blends; supports Cabernet Sauvignon, adding body and flesh; Saint-Estèphe uses the highest Merlot share on the Left Bank |
| Napa Valley, California | Warm, ripe blackberry/black raspberry; round, smooth, lush, jammy with sweet spice and vanilla from new oak; full-bodied |
| Sonoma, California | Plum, tea leaf, black cherry; cooler pockets (e.g. Bennett Valley) show less fruit and richness |
| Central Valley, Chile | A leading Merlot zone; third most-planted variety; much historic "Merlot" was actually Carménère until DNA profiling in the 1990s |
| Stellenbosch, South Africa | Chocolatey, glossy California-style; third most-planted red (~15% of red plantings); Merlot–Cabernet Franc Bordeaux blends common |
| Margaret River, Australia | Maritime climate moderates temperature; Bordeaux-blend focus; freshness alongside ripe fruit |
| Hawke's Bay, New Zealand | Gimblett Gravels sub-region; Bordeaux-style reds; ripens better with less green than Cabernet locally |

### Labelling terms

- **Right Bank** (Bordeaux) = Merlot-dominant blends (e.g. Saint-Émilion, Pomerol, Fronsac, Lalande-de-Pomerol).
- **Left Bank** (Bordeaux) = Cabernet Sauvignon-dominant; Merlot plays support.
- **Bordeaux AOC / Bordeaux Supérieur** = entry-level blends, often Merlot-majority.
- **Varietal labelling** (New World) = a single-variety "Merlot" label means ≥75–85% Merlot, depending on the country's rules.
- **Meritage** (USA) = a Bordeaux-variety blend with no single grape named on the label.
- **Gimblett Gravels** (Hawke's Bay, NZ) = a GI for premium Bordeaux-style reds.

## Implications

- **Blind-tasting tells:** soft, velvety, low-to-medium tannins (notably softer/less drying than Cabernet Sauvignon), medium acidity, plum and black-cherry fruit. If it's drying and herbaceous it may be cool-climate Merlot edging toward a Cabernet profile.
- **Earth and minerality** are unusually prominent in Merlot-based Bordeaux compared with other regions — a useful pointer to Right Bank Bordeaux.
- **Region → grape associations:** Saint-Émilion → Merlot (+ Cabernet Franc); Pomerol → Merlot (+ Cabernet Franc); "Right Bank" → Merlot-dominant.
- **Soil clue:** clay (Pomerol) → broader, more robust, opulent; limestone (Saint-Émilion) → fresher, more mineral.
- **New World decoder:** Napa/Stellenbosch → rounder, smoother, oak-driven; cooler sites (Sonoma's Bennett Valley) → leaner, more European.

## Misconceptions

- **"Merlot and Cabernet Sauvignon taste the same."** Merlot has thinner skins, fewer/softer tannins, higher sugar and lower acid, giving a plusher, less drying wine.
- **"Merlot is just a blending grape."** On Bordeaux's Right Bank it dominates the blend (~60% in Saint-Émilion, ~80% in Pomerol) and carries the wine.
- **"Merlot is always soft and low-tannin."** Pomerol's iron-clay soils and cool-climate sites can give it grippier tannins and a firmer backbone.
- **"All Chilean Merlot is Merlot."** Much of Chile's historic Merlot was actually Carménère, only separated by DNA profiling in the 1990s.
- **"Merlot is always a low-alcohol, light wine."** Late-harvested warm-climate Merlot is full-bodied and high in alcohol.
