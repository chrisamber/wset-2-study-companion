# Cabernet Sauvignon

## Analogy

Cabernet Sauvignon is the thick-skinned heavyweight of black grapes. The berries are small with notably thick skins and a high seed-to-fruit ratio (roughly 1:12) — and that skin is where everything comes from: deep dark colour, high tannin, and the staying power to age for decades. Think of the skin as the wine's armour: it's what makes young Cabernet feel firm and structured, and what lets it soften slowly into cedar and pencil-shavings with time. It also blends so naturally with oak and with Merlot that its most famous expression — the Bordeaux blend — is a team effort, not a solo act.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

| Attribute | Profile |
|-----------|---------|
| Colour | Deep, dark red/purple |
| Body | Full |
| Tannin | High (from thick skins) |
| Acidity | Noticeable; medium / medium-plus to medium-high |
| Alcohol | 13–15% typical; California often above the Bordeaux average of 12–13%, frequently over 14% |
| Core aromas/flavours | Blackcurrant (cassis), black cherry, plum, blackberry, cedar, cigar box, pencil shavings (especially aged), tobacco |

The dominant flavour compounds are **methoxypyrazines** (green/herbal notes) and **thiols** (blackcurrant/cassis). Pyrazine levels drop as grapes ripen in warmer conditions — which is why climate so strongly governs the style. The source notes that climate warmth is "more important than the soil" in determining ripeness and style.

### How climate shapes the style

| Climate | Resulting style |
|---------|-----------------|
| Cool | Vegetal, bell pepper, asparagus; more restrained fruit, pronounced herbal/mineral notes, higher acidity, tighter structure |
| Medium / moderate | Mint, black pepper, eucalyptus, blackcurrant |
| Hot / warm | Ripe black cherry, plum; riper, jammier fruit, softer tannins, often higher ABV (14%+); risk of jammy/over-ripe flavours |

### Key winemaking decisions

- **Oak ageing:** Cabernet has a noted affinity for oak. Oak softens the naturally high tannins (gradual oxidation) and adds vanilla and baking spice (also coconut/toast) that complement blackcurrant and tobacco. The classic vessel is the 225-litre Bordeaux **barrique**. French oak gives subtler flavour; American oak (especially new) gives more robust, less subtle oak character.
- **Maceration:** Long maceration (traditionally three weeks in Bordeaux) extracts colour, flavour and tannin; shorter maceration (a few days) makes more approachable wines.
- **Tannin management:** via oak ageing, fining agents (gelatin, egg whites), or micro-oxygenation.
- **Blending:** The "Bordeaux blend" is Cabernet Sauvignon + Merlot + Cabernet Franc, potentially with Malbec, Petit Verdot or Carménère. Varieties are typically fermented and aged separately, then blended before bottling.

### Key regions → style

| Region / GI | Style |
|-------------|-------|
| **Bordeaux Left Bank (Médoc / Haut-Médoc)**, France | Historic home; Cabernet-dominant blends on gravel, well-drained soils in a maritime climate. (Merlot dominates the Right Bank — Saint-Émilion, Pomerol — on clay/limestone.) |
| **Pauillac** | "Strong lead pencil scent"; Cabernet commonly 75%+ of blend; home to three First Growths (Mouton Rothschild, Latour, Lafite) |
| **Margaux** | "Aromas of violets"; lighter soils can lack colour, compensated with Petit Verdot |
| **Saint-Julien** | "Cedar and cigar boxes" |
| **Saint-Estèphe** | More mineral; higher clay gives more tannic wines |
| **Graves / Pessac-Léognan** | "Strong blackcurrant flavours"; more mineral; Pessac-Léognan is the northern, higher-quality appellation (Château Haut-Brion) |
| **Napa Valley**, California | Warm Mediterranean; strong black fruit, full body, often above 14%. Hillside AVAs (Diamond/Howell Mtn, Mt. Veeder, Spring Mtn) = intense; valley floor = fruitier, more approachable. Oakville, Rutherford, Stags Leap noted; "Rutherford dust" terroir |
| **Sonoma County**, California | Slightly cooler than Napa; more elegant, with anise and black olive notes |
| **Maipo Valley**, Chile | "Pervasive blackcurrant fruit and an earthy, dusty note"; tobacco, cedar; firm yet ripe tannins; Chilean heartland for the grape |
| **Colchagua / Curicó**, Chile | Warmer; rich fruit "perceived as sweet"; lower acidity, softer tannins, approachable young |
| **Aconcagua**, Chile | Ripe but closed, tight; needs bottle time |
| **Stellenbosch**, South Africa | Heavy, full-bodied; dark/ripe black fruit, savoury herbal notes, well-integrated tannins; most important SA red district |
| **Constantia**, South Africa | Cooler coastal site; herbal and minty |
| **Coonawarra**, Australia | Terra rossa soil (red clay over limestone); intense blackcurrant, pronounced mint/eucalyptus, fine-grained tannins; excellent ageing |
| **Margaret River**, Australia | Tightly structured, pronounced black fruit; more restraint than Coonawarra; moderated by Indian Ocean |
| **Barossa Valley**, Australia | Big, full-bodied; often blended with Shiraz (a distinctively Australian style) |
| **Clare Valley**, Australia | Cooler; more concentrated fruit |
| **Hawke's Bay**, New Zealand | Main NZ region; warm, sunny eastern coast; vibrant blackcurrant, subtle herbaceousness, refined structure; Gimblett Gravels the best ripening site |

### Labelling & classification terms

- **Bordeaux Wine Official Classification of 1855:** classifies Left Bank estates from Premier Cru (First Growth) to Cinquième Cru (Fifth Growth).
- **Cru Classé / Grand Cru Classé:** quality hierarchy terms used in Bordeaux labelling.
- **Meritage:** US designation for Bordeaux-style blends (requires BSWA approval).
- **Varietal labelling (USA):** a wine labelled as varietal Cabernet Sauvignon may contain up to 25% other grapes.
- Bordeaux legal maximum yield: 50 hl/ha (a *plafond limite de classement* permits higher yields in exceptional years).

## Implications

- **Blind-tasting tells:** deep colour + high tannin + full body + high alcohol + blackcurrant/cassis is the classic Cabernet signature. Green bell pepper/herbaceous notes point to a cooler climate or under-ripeness; jammy ripe fruit and 14%+ alcohol point to a hot climate (Napa, warmer Chile/Australia).
- **Region → grape associations:** Bordeaux **Left Bank** (Médoc, Pauillac, Margaux, Saint-Julien, Saint-Estèphe, Pessac-Léognan) = Cabernet-dominant; Right Bank = Merlot. Coonawarra (terra rossa, mint/eucalyptus) and Maipo Valley are strong Cabernet flags.
- **Ageing:** Bordeaux top estates soften after ~10 years and can improve a further decade+; California premium develops over 2–3 decades (most approachable after 2 years); Australian premium needs ~10 years; South Africa 6–8 years; New Zealand generally drunk young (keeps green herbal notes).
- **Service:** it's a full-bodied, high-tannin red — exactly the category where you'd offer a structured, age-worthy bottle and let oak and time do their softening work.

## Misconceptions

- **"Cabernet always tastes of green bell pepper."** Those are methoxypyrazine notes that fade as the grape ripens in warmer climates — hot-climate Cabernet shows ripe black fruit, not pepper.
- **"All Bordeaux is Cabernet-based."** Only the Left Bank is Cabernet-dominant; the Right Bank (Saint-Émilion, Pomerol) is Merlot-dominant.
- **"Varietal Cabernet is 100% Cabernet Sauvignon."** In the USA a wine labelled varietal Cabernet may legally include up to 25% other grapes.
- **"Soil matters more than climate for its style."** The source states climate warmth is "more important than the soil" in determining ripeness and style.
- **"American and French oak do the same thing."** French oak imparts more subtle flavours; American oak (especially new barrels) gives more robust, less subtle oak character.
