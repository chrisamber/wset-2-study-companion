# Pinot Noir

## Analogy

Pinot Noir is the thin-skinned, high-maintenance soprano of red grapes. Its skin is delicate, so the wine comes out pale and lightly built — closer to a garnet gemstone than the inky purple of a Cabernet. Because the voice is so fine, the slightest mishandling shows: it sunburns in the heat, rots in its tight clusters, and turns coarse if you extract too hard. Get the conditions right and it's transparent — it broadcasts where it was grown more faithfully than almost any other grape, which is exactly why Burgundy built its whole reputation on it.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

A thin-skinned black grape with low levels of phenolic compounds, giving "mostly lightly colored, medium-bodied and low-tannin wines." Colour when young is often compared to garnet — much lighter than other red wines.

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Colour | Light to medium ruby/garnet; paler than most reds |
| Body | Light to medium |
| Tannin | Low to medium; soft, elegant |
| Acidity | High |
| Primary aromas | Red cherry, raspberry, strawberry, redcurrant |
| Secondary/tertiary | Mushroom, forest floor, damp leaves, barnyard, earthy; with oak: vanilla, spice, toast |

Young wines show red fruit (cherry, raspberry, strawberry). With age it develops vegetal and "barnyard" aromas and, in good years, complex fruit and forest-floor flavours — often peaking 15–20 years after the vintage.

### How climate and winemaking shape the style

Pinot Noir is a **cool-climate** variety, grown mostly in cooler regions; its thin skin makes it ill-suited to heat. It is far less tolerant of harsh conditions than Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah, Merlot or Grenache, and is sensitive to wind, frost, cropping levels, soil and pruning. Its tightly packed clusters make it prone to rot, bunch rot, fungal disease and powdery mildew — so it is "a difficult variety to cultivate." Ideal soil is chalky clay; in warmer areas canopy management is crucial to guard against sunburn.

- **Cool climate** → cabbage, wet-leaf notes at the extreme; generally higher acid, lower alcohol, leaner and more savoury.
- **Medium/warmer climate** → riper strawberry, raspberry, cherry, plus mushroom and meaty notes; rounder, fuller, more alcohol.

In the winery it is highly **reflective of terroir** and sensitive to fermentation method and yeast strain. **Delicate extraction is essential** — over-extraction risks harsh tannin. Oak (new or used) is used depending on the desired style.

**Old World style (Burgundy):** earthier, savoury, forest floor, higher acid, lower alcohol, longer-lived; labelled by appellation, not variety.
**New World style:** fruit-forward, riper cherry/plum, rounder tannins, often more body and alcohol; varietal labelling. An emerging California/New Zealand style is more powerful and darker, tending toward Syrah in depth and alcohol.

### Key regions and their styles

| Region | Climate | Style |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Burgundy — Côte d'Or (Gevrey-Chambertin, Nuits-Saint-Georges, Beaune, Pommard) | Cool, continental | The archetype; savoury, earthy, "farmyard," mineral, complex, age-worthy |
| Champagne, France | Cool | Most-planted variety (38%); blended with Chardonnay and Pinot Meunier; also Blanc de Noirs |
| Sancerre (Loire), France | Cool | Light red and rosé; much lighter than Burgundy; refreshing served chilled |
| Alsace, France | Cool | Pinot-noir d'Alsace; like red Burgundy/Beaujolais but usually drunk chilled |
| Willamette Valley, Oregon | Cool, maritime | Same latitude as Burgundy; bridges Old/New World — structure plus fruit; lighter than California |
| Sonoma Coast / Russian River Valley, California | Cool, foggy | Bright acidity, aromatic florals, bright fruit, refined herbs/spice |
| Carneros, California | Cool, windy | Leaner, higher-acid; straddles Napa and Sonoma |
| Santa Barbara Co. / Sta. Rita Hills, California | Cool, maritime | Elegant, high-acid, red-fruit character |
| Casablanca Valley, Chile | Cool, Pacific fog | Fresh, fruity, refined tannins, refreshing acidity |
| Walker Bay, South Africa | Cool, Atlantic | Elegant, medium-bodied; oldest Pinot region in SA |
| Yarra Valley, Victoria | Cool, elevated | Elegant, lighter-bodied, red fruit, earthy |
| Mornington Peninsula, Victoria | Cool, maritime | Refined, silky |
| Martinborough, NZ (Wairarapa) | Warm days, cool nights | Earthy, structured; NZ pioneer region |
| Marlborough, NZ | Cool to moderate | Fruit-forward, aromatic |
| Central Otago, NZ | Continental, high-altitude | Darkest, most concentrated NZ Pinot; cherry, baking spice, cola; punchier tannins |

Germany calls it **Spätburgunder** ("Late Burgundian") and ranks third worldwide behind France and the USA (Baden, Pfalz, Ahr). Chile also grows it in Leyda Valley and Biobío.

### Labelling

In **Burgundy** (and France generally) Pinot Noir is labelled by **appellation, not by grape** — e.g. Gevrey-Chambertin, Nuits-Saint-Georges, Beaune, Pommard, or generic Bourgogne. New World bottles typically state "Pinot Noir" on the label.

## Implications

- **Blind-tasting tells:** pale garnet colour, high acid, low–medium soft tannin, and red-fruit-plus-earthy/forest-floor aromas point to Pinot Noir. Pale colour alone separates it from most other reds.
- **Region → grape associations to drill:** Burgundy red (Gevrey-Chambertin, Nuits-Saint-Georges, Beaune, Pommard) → Pinot Noir; Spätburgunder → Pinot Noir; Central Otago → Pinot Noir. Remember it is also a major Champagne grape.
- **Old vs New World contrast:** earthy/savoury/higher-acid Burgundy vs fruit-forward, rounder New World — a classic compare-two-wines exam framing.
- **Service note:** light-bodied reds like Pinot Noir (and Loire/Alsace examples) are often served lightly chilled and show best slightly below room temperature.

## Misconceptions

- **"Red wines are deeply coloured."** Pinot Noir is thin-skinned and naturally pale — garnet, much lighter than most reds.
- **"Burgundy red is a blend or a grape name on the label."** Burgundy reds are Pinot Noir labelled by appellation, not by variety.
- **"Pinot Noir is easy to grow anywhere."** It is a difficult, fussy variety — heat-sensitive, frost- and rot-prone, and far less tolerant than Cabernet, Syrah, Merlot or Grenache.
- **"More extraction makes better Pinot."** Delicate extraction is essential; over-extraction produces harsh tannin.
- **"Spätburgunder is a separate German grape."** Spätburgunder is simply the German name for Pinot Noir.
