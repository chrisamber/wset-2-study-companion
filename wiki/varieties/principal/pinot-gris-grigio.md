# Pinot Gris / Pinot Grigio

## Analogy

This is one grape wearing two hats, and the hat is printed on the label. Picture the same person dressed for two occasions: in a crisp linen shirt for a hot Italian lunch (**"Pinot Grigio"** — light, lean, citrussy, cold), or in a heavy wool jacket for a chilly Alsace evening (**"Pinot Gris"** — full, oily, spiced, richer). Same DNA, different outfit. When Zeal sees the name on a list, the spelling is telling her what style to expect before she even pulls the cork.

## Mechanics

### Grape identity

Pinot Gris and Pinot Grigio are the **same grape** — *Vitis vinifera*, a colour mutation of Pinot Noir (UC Davis found a near-identical DNA profile to Pinot Noir). The berries grow in small pinecone-shaped clusters, giving the "Pinot" prefix. Skin colour is a pinkish-grey, ranging to blue-grey or pinkish-brown, so finished wines can run from deep golden yellow through copper to a light pink, depending on skin contact and winemaking.

### The two-style split

The name on the label signals the intended style — a reliable market signal even though it is not legally enforced globally:

- **"Pinot Grigio" (Italian style):** grapes often **harvested early** to keep acidity high and minimise fruitiness. Result is light-bodied, lean, pale, dry, high in acidity, sometimes slightly spritzy (frizzante). Fermented and matured in **stainless steel** with no/minimal oak to preserve freshness — oak would add weight and vanilla that detract from the clean style.
- **"Pinot Gris" (Alsace style):** medium to full-bodied with an almost oily texture, moderate-to-low acidity, higher alcohol, richer and spicier. Can range dry → off-dry → fully sweet, and can age well.

### Style comparison table

| Feature | Pinot Gris (Alsace) | Light Italian (Veneto / Delle Venezie) | Premium Italian (Friuli-Venezia Giulia) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Body | Medium to full (oily texture) | Light | Light to medium |
| Acidity | Moderate to low | High | High to medium |
| Alcohol | Higher | Lower | — |
| Sweetness | Dry to off-dry (sweet styles exist) | Dry | Dry |
| Colour | Deep gold to amber | Pale straw | Pale straw to light gold |
| Key flavours | Ripe stone fruit, melon, mango, honey, beeswax, smoke, spice | Lemon, lime, green apple, pear, bitter-almond finish; neutral | Peach, almond, green apple, subtle florals |
| Winemaking | Lees work; minimal oak; botrytis for sweet styles | Stainless steel; early harvest | Stainless steel; sometimes skin contact/lees |
| Ageing | Can age (esp. VT/SGN) | Drink young | Young to medium |

### Key regions / GIs

| Region / GI | Style |
|---|---|
| **Alsace (France)** | One of Alsace's noble grapes (with Riesling, Gewürztraminer, Muscat). Cool climate + warm volcanic soils suit it. Medium-to-full, rich, floral, spicy; ripe stone fruit, tropical (melon, mango), honey, beeswax, smoke. Dry to fully sweet. |
| **Veneto / Delle Venezie (Italy)** | The large-volume everyday style: crisp, light, dry, high acidity. The Delle Venezie DOC (est. 2017) covers the Tre Venezie and is dedicated almost entirely to Pinot Grigio. |
| **Friuli-Venezia Giulia (Italy)** | Premium expressions — fuller body, delicate peach/almond/green apple, more aromatic complexity and structure than the bulk style. Colli Orientali del Friuli and Friuli Isonzo subregions noted. |
| **Alto Adige / Trentino (Italy)** | Cooler Alpine conditions; slightly more aromatic and structured than basic Veneto, but still in the lighter Italian style. |

### Labelling terms

- **"Pinot Grigio delle Venezie DOC"** — must be varietal Pinot Grigio meeting minimum quality standards; covers Veneto, Friuli-Venezia Giulia, and Trentino-Alto Adige.
- **Alsace late-harvest designations:** "Vendange Tardive" (late harvest, off-dry to sweet) and "Sélection de Grains Nobles" (botrytis-affected, very sweet).
- **Regional synonyms:** "Pinot gris" (France/Alsace), "Pinot grigio" (Italy), "Grauburgunder"/"Ruländer" (Germany), "Szürkebarát" (Hungary). In Australia, drier wines tend to be labelled "Pinot Grigio" and richer ones "Pinot Gris."

## Implications

- **The classic exam point:** one grape, two names, two styles. If a question gives you "Pinot Grigio," think light/lean/high-acid/Italian; "Pinot Gris" think fuller/richer/spicy/Alsace.
- **Region drill:** Alsace → fuller, noble grape; Veneto/Delle Venezie → bulk crisp style; Friuli-Venezia Giulia → premium Italian.
- **Winemaking matters:** stainless steel + early harvest = the fresh Italian style; lees work and botrytis = the richer/sweeter Alsace styles.
- Associate Alsace with its fellow noble grapes — Riesling, Gewürztraminer, Muscat — they often appear together in questions.

## Misconceptions

- **"Pinot Gris and Pinot Grigio are different grapes."** No — same grape; the name signals the intended style.
- **"Pinot Grigio is always bone-dry and neutral, full stop."** That's the bulk Veneto/Delle Venezie style; Friuli examples are fuller and more complex, and Alsace versions can be off-dry or sweet.
- **"It's a pure white grape."** Its skin is pinkish-grey (a Pinot Noir colour mutation), so wines can show copper or light pink hues with skin contact.
- **"Alsace Pinot Gris is always sweet."** It ranges from dry through off-dry to fully sweet; only Vendange Tardive and Sélection de Grains Nobles are the dedicated sweeter designations.
- **"Oak makes Italian Pinot Grigio better."** For the mainstream style, oak adds weight and vanilla that detract from the clean, neutral character — hence stainless steel.
