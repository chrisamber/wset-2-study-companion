# Riesling

## Analogy

Riesling is the grape with a built-in acid backbone — think of it like fresh-squeezed lime juice that you can sweeten as much or as little as you like. The sourness never goes away, so even when there's a lot of sugar in the glass it tastes refreshing rather than cloying. That high-acid spine is also why a bottle can age for decades and, with time, pick up its signature whiff of "petrol."

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

- **Colour:** white grape, originating in the Rhine Valley of western Germany.
- **Acidity:** naturally very high — one of the most acidic white grapes; preserved because Riesling does **not** typically undergo malolactic fermentation.
- **Body:** light to medium (Germany) through medium to full (Alsace, Pfalz, Australia).
- **Alcohol:** low in German styles (around 7.5–11%); higher in Alsace (~12%) and Australian dry styles.
- **Sugar:** the full spectrum — bone dry through lusciously sweet. Contrary to popular assumption, most Riesling made globally is dry or off-dry.
- **Primary aromas/flavours:** green apple, citrus (lime, lemon, grapefruit), peach, apricot, nectarine, gooseberry, floral (jasmine, rose, blossom), honey, ginger.
- **Aged aromas:** **petrol/kerosene** (from the compound TDN), plus toast, smoke, honey and beeswax.
- It is "terroir-expressive" — its character is strongly shaped by where it grows.

### The petrol (TDN) note

The petrol/kerosene aroma develops with bottle age from the compound TDN (1,1,6-trimethyl-1,2-dihydronaphthalene). It is promoted by ripe grapes from low-yield harvests, high sun exposure, water stress and high acidity. It is most common in aged Australian Riesling but is also characteristic of aged German Riesling. Some German producers see it as a fault and try to minimise it; experienced collectors treat it as a quality indicator.

### How climate and ripeness shape the style

- **Cool climate (e.g. Mosel):** apple and tree-fruit notes with pronounced acidity, often balanced by residual sugar; light, low-alcohol styles.
- **Warmer sites (Pfalz, Alsace, Austria):** citrus and peach character from later ripening; fuller body, higher alcohol, drier.
- **Australia (Clare/Eden):** thicker-skinned grapes (up to seven times the skin thickness of German-grown grapes) give an oily texture and a characteristic lime note; fermented bone dry.
- Higher ripeness underpins Germany's Prädikat ladder (below): riper must weight = a more elevated Prädikat, independent of how sweet the finished wine ends up.

### Key regions and their styles

| Region | Country | Style |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Mosel | Germany | Cool, slate soils, steep slopes; light, high-acid, apple/tree fruit; often off-dry to sweet; low alcohol |
| Rheingau | Germany | Rhine-facing slopes, slate; elegant dry to off-dry; structured, classic |
| Pfalz (Palatinate) | Germany | Warmer, drier; fuller body; citrus and peach; broad range including powerful dry styles |
| Alsace | France | Dry, higher alcohol (~12%); rich, round texture; intense citrus and white peach, floral, mineral |
| Clare Valley | Australia | Bone dry; powerful lime/lemon/grapefruit; high acid; ages to petrol |
| Eden Valley | Australia | Bone dry; lime and citrus; slightly more delicate and floral than Clare |

### German labelling — the two axes

German wine law works on two independent axes: **Prädikat = grape ripeness at harvest**; **sweetness term = the finished wine's sugar level**. So a *Spätlese Trocken* is from late-harvested grapes but fermented to dryness.

Quality tiers: **Qualitätswein (QbA)** is the basic level (51–72°Oe, min 7% alcohol, chaptalisation allowed). **Prädikatswein** is the superior tier — chaptalisation is **prohibited**, and the label carries a ripeness designation (Prädikat).

The six Prädikat levels (rising ripeness):

| Prädikat | Meaning | Typical style |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| Kabinett | Entry-level Prädikat, ripe grapes | Lightest; low alcohol; dry to off-dry |
| Spätlese | "Late harvest" (picked ≥7 days after normal) | Riper, more concentrated; dry to medium-sweet (not necessarily sweet) |
| Auslese | "Select harvest" — hand-selected ripe bunches | Often noble rot; semi-sweet to sweet (dry possible) |
| Beerenauslese (BA) | "Select berry harvest" — overripe, usually botrytised berries | Rich, sweet dessert wine; rare |
| Trockenbeerenauslese (TBA) | "Select dry berry harvest" — shrivelled botrytised berries | Honey-like, extremely rich and sweet; rarest |
| Eiswein | Grapes frozen on the vine (≤ −7 °C) | Intense acidity + sweetness; BA-level must weight; no noble rot |

Sweetness terms on the finished wine (independent of Prädikat):

| Term | Meaning | Residual sugar |
|------|---------|----------------|
| Trocken | Dry | Max ~4 g/L |
| Halbtrocken | Half-dry / off-dry | Max ~12 g/L |
| Feinherb | Off-dry (unofficial, unregulated) | Slightly sweeter than Halbtrocken |
| Lieblich / Mild | Semi-sweet | Higher RS |
| Süß / Edelsüß | Sweet | Very high RS |

### Aging potential

High acidity and pronounced fruit give exceptional ageing potential. Rough windows: dry 5–15 years; semi-sweet 10–20 years; sweet 10–30+ years. High-quality dry/off-dry examples have been enjoyable beyond 100 years.

## Implications

- **Blind-tasting tell:** very high acidity + pronounced floral/citrus/stone-fruit + (if aged) a petrol/kerosene note points strongly to Riesling. A naturally light body with low alcohol leans German; rounder, ~12% and bone dry leans Alsace; bone dry with pure lime leans Australian (Clare/Eden).
- **Reading the label:** Prädikat tells you ripeness, not sweetness — check for a separate sweetness term. Watch the trap: the "Trocken" inside *Trockenbeerenauslese* describes shrivelled grapes, not a dry wine (TBA is intensely sweet).
- **Service / food:** the sugar-acid balance makes Riesling very food-versatile and it especially excels with spicy food — high acidity refreshes the palate while residual sugar in sweeter styles tames the heat. Good with Thai, Indian and Chinese dishes, white fish, shellfish and soft cheeses.
- Fermentation is in temperature-controlled stainless steel (10–18 °C), the grape needs gentle handling at harvest, and cold stabilisation prevents tartaric crystals in bottle.

## Misconceptions

- **"All Riesling is sweet."** Most Riesling made globally is dry or off-dry; Alsace and Australian (Clare/Eden) examples are bone dry.
- **"Trockenbeerenauslese is a dry wine."** "Trocken" here refers to the shrivelled/desiccated berries — TBA is extremely sweet.
- **"A higher Prädikat means a sweeter wine."** Prädikat measures grape ripeness at harvest, not finished sweetness; a Spätlese can be fermented fully dry.
- **"The petrol smell is a fault."** It comes from TDN developing with age and is widely regarded as a quality indicator (though some German producers dislike it).
- **"Riesling is improved by oak and malolactic like Chardonnay."** Riesling typically avoids malolactic fermentation to keep its tart, high-acid character intact.
