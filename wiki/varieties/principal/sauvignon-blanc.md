# Sauvignon Blanc

## Analogy

Sauvignon Blanc is the wine equivalent of biting into a tart green apple just outside a freshly cut lawn — sharp, mouth-watering, and unmistakably *green*. Its very name comes from the French *sauvage* ("wild") and *blanc* ("white"), and that wild green streak is the whole identity: high acidity and herbaceous, grassy aromas that jump out of the glass. Where most whites whisper, classic unoaked Sauvignon Blanc shouts.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

| Attribute | Sauvignon Blanc |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Skin / wine colour | Green-skinned; white wine |
| Acidity | **High** — the defining structural feature |
| Sugar | Low residual sugar in most styles (dry) |
| Body | Light to medium |
| Alcohol | Typically moderate |
| Signature aromas | Gooseberry, cut grass, green herbs (nettles, green capsicum/bell pepper, asparagus), citrus, elderflower, passion fruit |

The green, grassy notes come from chemicals called **methoxypyrazines** (asparagus, gooseberry, green characters in cooler climates), while **thiols** drive riper notes like passion fruit and boxwood.

### How climate shapes the style

| Climate | What happens | Resulting flavours |
|---------|--------------|--------------------|
| **Cool** (Loire, Marlborough, Elgin, Casablanca, Adelaide Hills) | Acidity preserved; methoxypyrazine "green" character pronounced | Gooseberry, cut grass, green herbs, citrus (grapefruit, lime), elderflower, passion fruit |
| **Warm** | Develops tropical notes but risks losing aromatic intensity | Tropical fruit, subtler grapefruit and tree-fruit notes; herbaceousness softer |

### Key winemaking decisions

- **Unoaked (dominant style):** fermented in stainless steel at cool temperatures to preserve aromatics, freshness and high acidity. This is the approach in Sancerre, Pouilly-Fumé, Touraine, Marlborough, Casablanca, Elgin/Constantia and Adelaide Hills. Across the world, including in France, the grape is hardly ever oaked — winemakers prefer to show its bright, fruity character.
- **Oaked style (Fumé Blanc / Bordeaux Blanc):** oak ageing in barriques rounds out flavours, adds texture and complexity, and softens the naturally high acidity, giving a richer, rounder wine. Used in Bordeaux/Graves/Pessac-Léognan and in California Fumé Blanc.
- **Fumé Blanc:** a marketing term coined by Robert Mondavi in California in 1968 to rebrand the variety using barrel ageing; now used broadly across California for oak-aged versions.
- **Fermentation temperature:** French winemakers prefer warmer fermentations (around 16–18 °C) to bring out mineral flavours; New World winemakers prefer slightly cooler temperatures to emphasise fruit and tropical flavours.
- **Skin contact:** prolonged skin contact intensifies pungency and sharpness; minimal contact can preserve ageing potential.
- **Blending with Sémillon:** in Bordeaux, Sémillon is the traditional partner (sometimes with a little Muscadelle) — oak-aged for a richer dry white. In Margaret River (Australia) the Sauvignon Blanc–Sémillon blend is also common and may be oaked or unoaked. In Sauternes/Barsac, Sauvignon Blanc joins botrytised Sémillon to make sweet dessert wine.

### Key regions and their styles

| Region (GI) | Style |
|-------------|-------|
| **Sancerre / Pouilly-Fumé** (Loire) | Flinty, mineral, high acid; grass, green herbs, citrus; unoaked stainless steel. Flinty/limestone soils give a "smoky, gunflint" character — the origin of *Fumé* in Pouilly-Fumé |
| **Touraine** (Loire) | Lighter, more straightforward varietal style; lower price tier |
| **Bordeaux / Graves / Pessac-Léognan** | Blended with Sémillon; oak-fermented/aged; richer texture, herbal + tropical fruit |
| **Napa Valley** (California) | Often labelled Fumé Blanc; oak-aged; richer, rounder, tropical fruit. Most prestigious California sub-region for the variety |
| **Casablanca Valley** (Chile) | Cool maritime Pacific influence; preserves high acidity and aromatic intensity; citrus and green herb |
| **Elgin / Constantia** (South Africa) | Cool climate (Constantia coastal, Elgin high-altitude); crisp, aromatic, citrus and green herb — similar to cool-climate Loire |
| **Adelaide Hills / Margaret River** (Australia) | More ripe — white peach, lime, slightly higher acidity than NZ; Margaret River often blends with Sémillon |
| **Marlborough** (New Zealand) | Intense passion fruit, green capsicum, gooseberry, cut grass; unoaked stainless steel. Globally prominent from the 1980s |

### Labelling terms

- **Sauvignon Blanc** — standard designation used worldwide.
- **Fumé Blanc** — California term for oak-aged versions (originated with Robert Mondavi, 1968).
- **Blanc Fumé** — French form; *Pouilly Blanc Fumé* is a synonym for Pouilly-Fumé.
- **Sav Blanc** — informal Australian abbreviation.
- In France, the **AOC name** (Sancerre, Pouilly-Fumé, Touraine, Bordeaux Blanc, Pessac-Léognan) appears on the label rather than the grape variety.

## Implications

- **Region → grape association:** Sancerre → Sauvignon Blanc; Pouilly-Fumé → Sauvignon Blanc; Marlborough → Sauvignon Blanc. These appellation names appear on the label *instead* of the grape, so learn them as direct equivalences.
- **Blind-tasting tells:** very high acidity plus pronounced green/grassy, gooseberry and green-capsicum aromas point to cool-climate unoaked Sauvignon Blanc. Pronounced passion fruit and cut grass especially suggest Marlborough.
- **Oaked vs unoaked clue:** a fuller, rounder texture with softer acidity (and oak influence) suggests a Bordeaux Blanc / Pessac-Léognan or a California Fumé Blanc rather than Sancerre or Marlborough.
- **Sémillon is the partner to remember** for the oaked Bordeaux dry whites, the Margaret River blends, and (botrytised) the sweet Sauternes.

## Misconceptions

- **"Sauvignon Blanc is usually oaked."** No — it is hardly ever oaked worldwide; stainless-steel, unoaked is the dominant style. Oak is the exception (Bordeaux/Pessac-Léognan and California Fumé Blanc).
- **"Fumé Blanc is a different grape."** It is just a marketing name for (usually oak-aged) Sauvignon Blanc, coined by Robert Mondavi in 1968.
- **"The smoky 'Fumé' in Pouilly-Fumé comes from oak."** No — it refers to the flinty/limestone soils believed to impart a smoky, gunflint character; these wines are typically unoaked.
- **"A Sancerre label will say Sauvignon Blanc."** In France the AOC name appears instead of the grape, so Sancerre and Pouilly-Fumé labels generally don't state the variety.
- **"Warmer climates make better Sauvignon Blanc."** Warmer regions add tropical notes but risk losing the variety's aromatic intensity; cool climates best preserve its signature acidity and green aromas.
