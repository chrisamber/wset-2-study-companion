# Chardonnay

## Analogy

Chardonnay is a blank canvas, not a painting. The grape itself is notably neutral — its flavours come largely from where it grows (terroir) and what the winemaker does (oak, malolactic fermentation, lees) rather than from anything loud in the grape. That's why a steely, mineral Chablis and a buttery, tropical Napa Chardonnay can be the *same* grape: the canvas is identical, the painting is not. When you taste Chardonnay blind, you're really reading climate and winemaking choices.

## Mechanics

### Grape characteristics

| Attribute | Chardonnay |
|-----------|-----------|
| Colour | White (green-skinned grape); pale lemon → gold in glass with oak/age |
| Body | Light to full (depends on climate) |
| Acidity | Medium to high (cool climate) → medium (warm climate) |
| Tannin | None (white wine) |
| Alcohol | Medium to high |

Chardonnay originated in the Burgundy region of east-central France and is a cross between Pinot Noir and Gouais Blanc. It is one of the most widely planted varieties (210,000 ha worldwide).

### How climate shapes style

| Climate | Body / acidity | Typical aromas & flavours |
|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Cool** (Chablis, Willamette, Carneros) | Light–medium body, high acidity | Green apple, lemon, lime, pear, green plum, mineral/flint, oyster shell |
| **Moderate** | Medium body, moderate acidity | Citrus, peach, nectarine, melon |
| **Warm** | Full body, lower acidity | Fig, mango, pineapple, banana — tropical fruit |

### Key winemaking decisions

The grape is neutral, so winemaking is decisive. Three choices matter most:

| Technique | Effect on the wine |
|-----------|--------------------|
| Stainless steel / no oak | Preserves freshness, green/citrus fruit, high acidity (e.g. Chablis) |
| Oak fermentation/ageing | Vanilla, caramel, smoke, toast, spice; adds body — extent depends on charring |
| Malolactic fermentation (MLF) | Converts sharp malic acid to softer lactic acid; buttery, creamy, yogurt/popcorn notes; softer acidity, reduced freshness |
| Lees ageing (bâtonnage) | Stirring lees in barrel adds bready/biscuit/nutty complexity and texture; reduces freshness |

MLF and heavy oak/lees work are standard in the Côte de Beaune and much of California; they are uncommon in Chablis and unoaked styles.

### Labelling & classification (Burgundy)

Burgundy's hierarchy runs from regional → village → Premier Cru → Grand Cru:

- **Grand Cru** — highest classification (Côte d'Or and Chablis); specific named vineyards. Chablis has seven Grand Cru vineyards (Blanchot, Bougros, Les Clos, Grenouilles, Preuses, Valmur, Vaudésir) over ~100 ha; the Côte de Beaune has Montrachet, Corton-Charlemagne.
- **Premier Cru** — one rank below; named vineyard on the label (e.g. Fourchaume, Montée de Tonnerre in Chablis; Perrières in Meursault). Chablis has ~40.
- **Village wine** — appellation-level, e.g. Meursault, Puligny-Montrachet.
- **Regional wine** — e.g. Bourgogne Blanc, Mâcon-Villages.
- **New World** — varietal labelling ("Chardonnay") is standard; no equivalent classification hierarchy.

## Implications

- **Chablis vs oaked Napa is the classic contrast.** Same grape: Chablis is cool-climate, unoaked, high-acid, mineral/green-apple/lemon; warm-climate Napa is full-bodied, oaked, buttery (MLF) with tropical fruit and vanilla. If a "Chardonnay" question hinges on butter + vanilla + tropical fruit, think warm climate + oak + MLF; if it hinges on flint + green apple + high acid, think Chablis.
- **Blind-tasting tells:** white wine with no tannin, medium-to-high acidity, and oak/buttery/nutty notes points to Chardonnay; the *degree* of oak, MLF and tropical fruit tells you climate and winemaking, not a different grape.
- **Côte de Beaune is the middle ground** — oaked and MLF-treated, but cooler-climate fruit purity sits between lean Chablis and ripe warm-climate styles. Mâcon is unoaked but warmer than Chablis.
- **Service:** medium–full-bodied/oaked Chardonnay is served around 10–13 °C (see [[wine-serving-temperature]]).

## Misconceptions

- **"Chardonnay always tastes of butter and vanilla."** That comes from MLF and oak — unoaked Chablis is lean, mineral and crisp with none of it.
- **"All Chardonnay is heavily oaked."** Chablis and Mâcon are typically unoaked or only lightly oaked; oak is a choice, not a given.
- **"The grape has a strong, distinctive flavour."** Chardonnay is notably neutral; its character comes mostly from terroir and winemaking.
- **"Chablis is a grape."** Chablis is a place (cool-climate Burgundy); the grape is Chardonnay.
- **"Buttery notes mean residual sugar / sweetness."** The creamy, buttery quality comes from malolactic fermentation softening acidity, not from sugar — these are dry wines.
