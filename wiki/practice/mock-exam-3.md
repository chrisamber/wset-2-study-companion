# WSET Level 2 Award in Wines — Mock Exam 3

**Format (matches the real exam):** 50 multiple-choice questions · 60 minutes · one correct answer each · 1 mark per question · **no marks deducted for wrong answers** · pass = 55% (28/50). Merit ≥ 70% (35), Distinction ≥ 85% (43).

**Weighting of this paper (same as the real exam):** LO1 = Q1–5 · LO2 = Q6–9 · LO3 = Q10–28 · LO4 = Q29–40 · LO5 = Q41–46 · LO6 = Q47–50.

> **Integrity note:** Every question and answer is drawn from this pack's RAG corpus ([[wset-l2-wines-rag]]) and the WSET L2 specification. No facts were invented; all distractors are real WSET L2 terms. This is a fresh paper — it does not repeat the questions in [[mock-exam]] or [[mock-exam-2]], so use all three together. ## Questions

**1.** Which part of the grape supplies most of the sugar, acids and water that become wine?
a) The skins
b) The seeds
c) The stems
d) The pulp

**2.** Moving from a cool climate to a warm climate, the same grape variety will tend to give a wine that is:
a) Higher in acidity and lighter in body
b) Riper in fruit, higher in alcohol and lower in acidity
c) Lower in alcohol with more herbaceous notes
d) Unchanged in style

**3.** Compared with a PDO (e.g. a French AOC), a PGI (e.g. a French IGP / Vin de Pays) generally covers:
a) A smaller area with stricter rules
b) Exactly the same rules under a different name
c) A larger area with less restrictive rules
d) Only wines made outside the EU

**4.** Which is the top-tier geographical-indication category in Italy?
a) DOCG
b) IGT
c) DOC
d) Vino de la Tierra

**5.** On a French label, the term "Vendanges Tardives" tells you the wine was made from grapes that were:
a) Grown on very old vines
b) Harvested late (late harvest)
c) Affected by noble rot
d) Picked while frozen for icewine

**6.** A red wine gets its colour and tannin because, unlike a white wine, it is fermented:
a) Without the grape skins
b) With the grape skins
c) Only in stainless steel
d) At very cool temperatures

**7.** White wines are often fermented at cool temperatures in order to:
a) Extract more tannin
b) Add oak flavour
c) Preserve fresh, fruity aromas
d) Raise the alcohol level

**8.** Chaptalisation is the practice of:
a) Adding acidity to the must
b) Removing alcohol from the finished wine
c) Adding tannin during ageing
d) Adding sugar to the must to raise the finished alcohol

**9.** As a white wine matures in the bottle over several years, its colour and flavours typically develop towards:
a) Purple colour with fresh berry fruit
b) A paler colour with more fizz
c) Green colour with grassy aromas
d) A deeper gold colour with honey and nutty notes

**10.** Chile's Central Valley and the Right Bank of Bordeaux are both strongholds of which black grape?
a) Cabernet Sauvignon

**11.** Chile's Maipo Valley is especially noted for which black grape?

**12.** Which aroma is most typical of Cabernet Sauvignon grown in cooler conditions or picked less ripe?
a) Lychee

**13.** Crozes-Hermitage in the Northern Rhône is made from which black grape?
a) Grenache
b) Pinot Noir

**14.** Which aroma is most associated with cool-climate Syrah (e.g. Northern Rhône) rather than warm-climate Shiraz?

**18.** A rich white Burgundy from Meursault gets its buttery, creamy character mainly from:

**19.** Comparing a Chablis with an oaked California Chardonnay, which statement is correct?

## Answer key & basis

*(Basis = the Learning Outcome and the [[wset-l2-wines-rag]] content each answer is grounded in.)*

| Q | Ans | Basis |
|---|-----|-------|
| 1 | **d** | LO1 — pulp gives sugar, acids, water (and flavours); skins give colour/tannin/flavour; seeds & stems give tannin. |
| 2 | **b** | LO1 — as climate warms, grapes ripen more: sugar (→ alcohol) up, acidity down, riper fruit; cool climates keep acidity, lower alcohol. |
| 3 | **c** | LO1 — PDO = tighter, smaller, place-specific rules; PGI = broader area, looser rules (France: AOC/AOP = PDO; IGP/Vin de Pays = PGI). |
| 4 | **a** | LO1 — Italian hierarchy: DOCG (top) → DOC (= PDO); IGT (= PGI). "Vino de la Tierra" is the Spanish PGI term. |
| 5 | **b** | LO1 — labelling terms: Late Harvest / Vendanges Tardives. (Old Vine = Vieilles Vignes; Noble Rot = botrytis; Icewine = Eiswein.) |
| 6 | **b** | LO2 — red ferments *with* the skins (extracting colour and tannin); white ferments without skins (or only brief contact). |
| 7 | **c** | LO2 — cool fermentation preserves fresh, fruity aromas (typical for whites); warm fermentation extracts more (typical for reds). |
| 8 | **d** | LO2 — chaptalisation = adding sugar before/during fermentation to raise the finished alcohol; a separate adjustment can correct acidity. |
| 9 | **d** | LO2 — bottle ageing: white deepens towards gold, fresh fruit → honey/nutty notes. (Purple→garnet/brown describes red.) |
| 10 | **b** | LO3 — Merlot → Bordeaux incl. Right Bank (Saint-Émilion, Pomerol); New World incl. Chile (Central Valley). |
| 11 | **a** | LO3 — Cabernet Sauvignon → Chile (Central Valley, **Maipo Valley**, Colchagua). |
| 12 | **c** | LO3 — Cabernet Sauvignon shows green bell pepper when less ripe / cooler; blackcurrant and (with oak) cedar when riper. |
| 13 | **d** | LO3 — Syrah → Northern Rhône PDOs: Crozes-Hermitage, Hermitage, Côte-Rôtie. |
| 14 | **c** | LO3 — cool-climate Syrah: black pepper, smoke; warm-climate Shiraz (e.g. Barossa): riper, spicier, jammy fruit. |
| 15 | **a** | LO3 — Pinot Noir → Burgundy PDOs incl. Beaune and Pommard (also Gevrey-Chambertin, Nuits-Saint-Georges). |
| 16 | **b** | LO3 — Pinot Noir → South Africa (Walker Bay), Australia (Yarra Valley, Mornington Peninsula). |
| 17 | **a** | LO3 — Chardonnay → Burgundy PDOs incl. Mâcon and Pouilly-Fuissé (also Chablis, Meursault, Puligny-Montrachet). |
| 18 | **b** | LO3 — Chardonnay's buttery, creamy style comes from oak and malolactic conversion (warm/oaked styles such as Meursault). |
| 19 | **a** | LO3 — Chardonnay spans lean, mineral, unoaked Chablis (cool) to ripe, oaked, fuller styles (warmer regions, e.g. California). |
| 20 | **d** | LO3 — Sauvignon Blanc → Loire (incl. Touraine); South Africa (Elgin, Constantia). |
| 21 | **a** | LO3/LO4 — Sauvignon Blanc → Bordeaux/Graves/Pessac-Léognan, classically blended with Sémillon (also a Bordeaux grape). |
| 22 | **b** | LO3 — Riesling → Germany: Mosel, Rheingau, Pfalz. |
| 23 | **d** | LO3 — Riesling: high acid, floral/citrus/stone fruit, petrol with age; styles dry → lusciously sweet. |
| 24 | **b** | LO3 — Pinot Gris/Grigio → Italy PDOs: Delle Venezie, Friuli-Venezia Giulia. |
| 25 | **a** | LO3 — Riesling reaches outstanding quality in **Germany** (Mosel, Rheingau, Pfalz) and **Australia** (Clare Valley, Eden Valley). |
| 26 | **b** | LO3 (labelling) — Burgundy ladder: Régionale → Village → **Premier Cru** → Grand Cru. |
| 27 | **a** | LO3 (labelling) — Bordeaux terms: Bordeaux, Bordeaux Supérieur, **Cru Bourgeois**, Grand Cru Classé (Médoc). |
| 28 | **b** | LO3 (labelling) — German dryness terms: Trocken = dry; **Halbtrocken = off-dry**. |
| 29 | **a** | LO4 — Tempranillo ageing ladder: **Joven** (little/no oak) → Crianza → Reserva → Gran Reserva. |
| 30 | **b** | LO4 — Barbera → Barbera d'Asti (Italy): high acid, low–medium tannin, juicy red fruit. |
| 31 | **a** | LO4 — Sangiovese terms: Classico = historic heartland; **Riserva = extended ageing** before release. |
| 32 | **c** | LO4 — Montepulciano d'Abruzzo is made from the **grape** Montepulciano (not the Tuscan town of the same name). |
| 33 | **b** | LO4 — Corvina → Valpolicella; **Amarone = dried-grape, fermented dry** (Recioto = dried-grape sweet). |
| 34 | **d** | LO4 — **Zinfandel** (USA, California) = **Primitivo** (Italy, Puglia): ripe, jammy, high alcohol. |
| 35 | **a** | LO4 — Carmenère → Chile (Central Valley): herbaceous, red/black fruit. |
| 36 | **b** | LO4 — Albariño → Spain (Rías Baixas): high acid, citrus/stone fruit, saline. |
| 37 | **c** | LO4 — Viognier → Northern Rhône (Condrieu): aromatic, peach/apricot, full body, low acid. |
| 38 | **a** | LO4 — Cortese → Gavi (Italy): light, crisp, citrus. |
| 39 | **d** | LO4 — Garganega → Soave / Soave Classico (Italy); labelling terms Classico, Recioto. |
| 40 | **c** | LO4 — Sémillon → Bordeaux, **Sauternes** (sweet, botrytised). (Furmint → Tokaj; Chenin → Vouvray.) |
| 41 | **b** | LO5 — tank (Charmat) method: 2nd fermentation in a pressurised tank, preserving fresh primary fruit/floral (Prosecco/Glera, Asti/Moscato). |
| 42 | **a** | LO5 — Méthode Cap Classique = South Africa's traditional-method (bottle-fermented) sparkling. |
| 43 | **c** | LO5 — sparkling sweetness terms: **Brut = dry**; Demi-Sec = medium-sweet. |
| 44 | **d** | LO5 — **timing is the key distinction:** Sherry fortified *after* fermentation (dry base); Port fortified *to interrupt* fermentation (leaves sweetness). Option c reverses them. |
| 45 | **b** | LO5 — Fino is aged biologically **under flor** (a yeast veil) → fresh, saline; Oloroso ages oxidatively → nutty. |
| 46 | **c** | LO5 — Port styles: **Ruby** (and Reserve Ruby) = youngest, fruity, deep-coloured; Tawny = long oxidative cask ageing (brown, nutty). |
| 47 | **d** | LO6 — service temperatures: medium- and full-bodied reds = room temperature; sweet/sparkling = well chilled. |
| 48 | **b** | LO6 — chilling raises perceived acidity/tannin and suppresses fruit/sweetness; warmth raises perceived alcohol and aromatics. |
| 49 | **c** | LO6 — preserving opened wine: vacuum system (pump out air) or blanket system (inert gas over the wine). |
| 50 | **b** | LO6 — salt and acid in food make wine seem softer, sweeter and more fruity (easier); sweet/umami food makes wine seem harder. |
