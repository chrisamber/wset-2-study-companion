| 1 | **b** | LO1 — grape-ripening-and-climate-source.md: 'Fruit Set — Follows flowering almost immediately' and spec 'Stages of grape formation: flowering, fruit set, véraison.' Fruit set follows flowering; véraison and harvest come later, leaf fall is end of cycle. |
| 2 | **c** | LO1 — grape-ripening-and-climate-source.md (Véraison): 'Herbaceous aromas — such as methoxypyrazines, which give unripe grapes a green, vegetal or bell-pepper character — degrade during this period.' Anthocyanins build (colour), tartaric acid is retained, tannins soften but don't cause the green character. |
| 3 | **a** | LO1 — grape-ripening-and-climate-source.md summary table: 'Cool | High [acidity] | Low / Light [alcohol/body].' Warm, hot and Mediterranean climates give lower acidity and fuller body / higher alcohol. |
| 4 | **b** | LO1 — grape-ripening-and-climate-source.md (Altitude): 'Higher altitude = lower temperatures ... greater diurnal temperature variation. Can produce grapes with both ripeness and retained acidity.' The other options misstate the temperature, sunlight and sugar effects. |
| 5 | **b** | LO1 — grape-ripening-and-climate-source.md (Weather Hazards): 'Frost: Spring frost during bud break and early growth can destroy new shoots and reduce or eliminate yield.' Drought is heat/water stress, autumn rain dilutes juice at harvest, and noble rot is a beneficial fungus, not a spring shoot hazard. |
| 6 | **b** | LO2 — winemaking source/spec: rosé is made by short maceration: dark skins left in contact with the juice just long enough to extract the desired colour, then pressed and fermented as a white. Blending red into white is less common; full skin-contact fermentation makes red; no skin contact makes white. |
| 7 | **c** | LO2 — fermentation source/spec: fermentation can be stopped early (leaving residual sugar) by chilling the must to stun the yeast, adding SO2, sterile filtering, or adding high alcohol. Acidification adjusts acidity, MLF softens acidity, and lees stirring adds texture — none leave sweetness. |
| 8 | **b** | LO2 — malolactic/lees source: keeping wine on the lees (sur lie) enhances body and flavour persistence and adds palate softness; stirring the lees (batonnage) increases contact, adding creaminess and savoury complexity. Tannin/colour come from skins; acidity is not raised by lees contact; fining (not batonnage) clarifies. |
| 9 | **b** | LO2 — bottle ageing (spec: red wine bottle ageing changes colour, tannin, aromas/flavours): with age red wine loses its youthful purple/ruby intensity and develops brick/brown tints (while tannins soften and aromas develop). Green-to-gold describes white wine ageing; the others reverse the direction. |
| 10 | **a** | LO3 — cabernet-sauvignon-source.md: methoxypyrazines give green/herbal (bell pepper) notes and decrease as grapes ripen in warmer conditions; cool-climate Cabernet shows vegetal/bell pepper character. |
| 11 | **c** | LO3 — cabernet-sauvignon-source.md: Coonawarra (South Australia) famous for terra rossa soil, intense blackcurrant, pronounced minty/eucalyptus notes, fine-grained tannins. |
| 12 | **b** | LO3 — merlot-source.md: Merlot has thinner skin and fewer tannins than Cabernet Sauvignon, giving softer, velvety, plush texture; medium tannins, medium-full body. |
| 13 | **d** | LO3 — merlot-source.md: in Pomerol Merlot is ~80% of the blend, usually blended with Cabernet Franc (Right Bank pattern Merlot + Cabernet Franc). |
| 14 | **a** | LO3 — merlot-source.md: Merlot ripens ~2 weeks earlier than Cabernet Sauvignon (copes in cooler regions) and its softness/fleshiness make it popular for blending with the sterner, later-ripening Cabernet Sauvignon. |
| 15 | **c** | LO3 — syrah-shiraz-source.md: up to 20% Viognier (a white grape) is permitted and co-fermented with Syrah in Côte-Rôtie for aromatic contribution. |
| 16 | **b** | LO3 — syrah-shiraz-source.md: hot-climate Barossa Shiraz is full-bodied, jammy (blackberry/plum), licorice/anise, high alcohol, softer tannin; contrasts with the cooler Northern Rhône's more tannic, peppery, restrained style. |
| 17 | **b** | LO3 — pinot-noir-source.md: Pinot Noir is thin-skinned with tightly packed clusters making it susceptible to rot/fungal disease; less tolerant of harsh conditions than Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah or Merlot; a cool-climate variety. |
| 18 | **b** | LO3 — chardonnay-source.md: Chablis is the leanest, cool-climate Chardonnay expression with high acidity, minerality and green apple/lemon/flint, typically unoaked. Distractors describe oaked Burgundy, warm-climate Chardonnay, and Alsace Gewurztraminer. |
| 19 | **c** | LO3 — sauvignon-blanc-source.md: 'Fumé Blanc' is a California marketing term (coined by Robert Mondavi, 1968) for oak-aged Sauvignon Blanc. |

| 21 | **b** | LO3 — riesling-source.md: Alsace Riesling is dry with higher alcohol (~12%), greater roundness and fuller body, versus the light, low-alcohol, often off-dry Mosel style. |
